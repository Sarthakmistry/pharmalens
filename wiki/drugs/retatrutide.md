### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis of 12 articles found that GLP-1RAs, including retatrutide (MD 3.46), were associated with an increase in heart rate in non-diabetic individuals with overweight or obesity. Orforglipron 36 mg showed the most pronounced increase (MD 9.29), while tirzepatide 5 mg showed the least (MD 0.52). (2026-04-12)
A meta-analysis of 137 trials involving 56,683 patients concluded that retatrutide (12 mg qw) was the most effective treatment for body weight reduction (mean difference = -26.56%). It also significantly improved glycemic control, with tirzepatide (10 mg qw) and oral orforglipron (10 mg qd) being most effective for HbA1c reduction. (2026-04-12)
A meta-analysis of 14 RCTs demonstrated that retatrutide achieved the greatest weight reduction versus placebo (MD -13.44 kg; 95% CI [-18.38, -8.51]) and the largest, statistically significant HbA1c reduction among several dual/triple agonists. However, retatrutide was associated with comparatively lower tolerability, while mazdutide showed the most favorable tolerability profile. (2026-04-12)
A meta-analysis of 25 RCTs involving 2600 patients showed that GLP-1RAs significantly reduced liver fat content (LFC) by 5.21% over a median of 24 weeks, with retatrutide displaying the most obvious treatment effects. These drugs also induced significant histological improvements in steatosis, hepatocellular ballooning, and lobular inflammation in MASLD and MASH patients, without worsening fibrosis or causing drug-related adverse liver effects. (2026-04-12)
A meta-analysis of 90,977 participants indicated that triple receptor agonists, such as retatrutide, significantly reduced systolic blood pressure (SBP) by 6.6 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) by 2.1 mmHg. Incretin-based therapies did not increase the risk of hypoglycemia or pancreatitis, and greater weight loss was significantly associated with a larger reduction in blood pressure. (2026-01-01)
Post-hoc analyses of two phase 2 trials revealed that retatrutide (8 and 12 mg in T2D; 1, 4, 8, 12 mg in obesity/overweight) reduced ANGPTL3/8 levels, which paralleled reductions in triglyceride (TG) and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C). This suggests that retatrutide's GCGR agonism may contribute to improved lipid profiles. (2025-10-01)
A meta-analysis of 62 studies (66,232 patients) found a significantly increased risk of pancreatitis with GLP-1 RAs (RR: 1.44), though this was not significant when stratified by background medications. No significant overall association with pancreatic cancer was observed, but a slight increase in risk was noted with background medications (RR: 1.85), which was deemed "likely minimal." (2025-09-01)
A meta-analysis of 29,506 adults indicated that retatrutide and dual agonists achieved equivalent mean weight loss (-11.0 kg), surpassing GLP-1RAs (-9.0 kg). Retatrutide particularly excelled at achieving ≥ 15% weight loss (OR 54.6). However, retatrutide was also associated with the highest adverse event (AE) risk among the compared treatments. (2025)
An RCT involving 189 participants with type 2 diabetes showed that retatrutide 8 mg (pooled) significantly reduced total body fat mass by 26.1% from baseline compared to placebo and dulaglutide. Gastrointestinal events were the most frequently reported adverse events, but no deaths occurred. The study noted that a greater proportion of lean mass was not lost despite overall increased weight loss. (2025-01-01)
An RCT in 275 adults with type 2 diabetes found that retatrutide doses of ≥4 mg significantly reduced overall appetite, hunger, and prospective food consumption compared to placebo. Higher doses (8 and 12 mg) improved Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition, and 12 mg increased Dietary Restraint, with these changes correlating significantly with body weight reductions. (2025-01-01)

### Competitive position
Retatrutide, as a triple agonist, continues to demonstrate superior efficacy in weight reduction and shows promising effects in reducing liver fat content in MASLD/MASH, making it a strong contender in the obesity and type 2 diabetes markets. While it excels in efficacy, its competitive position is somewhat tempered by a higher risk of adverse events, including increased heart rate and comparatively lower tolerability, which could influence patient and physician preference despite its strong efficacy.
