### Clinical evidence
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis involving 80,670 participants compared various antidiabetic agents against placebo for composite renal outcome, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). The primary result showed [[dapagliflozin]] 10 mg showing the greatest efficacy (OR 0.55, 95% CI 0.42-0.72; high-certainty evidence) for composite renal outcomes. Secondary results indicated that [[canagliflozin]] 100-300 mg significantly reduced UACR, whereas [[dapagliflozin]] had no effect. None of the novel antidiabetic agents significantly altered eGFR, and DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. The study concluded: In patients with T2DM and CKD, SGLT2 inhibitors provide the most consistent renal protection, while GLP-1 receptor agonists offer additional but variable benefits. [[dapagliflozin]] showed the greatest efficacy, and [[canagliflozin]] most strongly reduced albuminuria, highlighting meaningful heterogeneity across agents. DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. Overall, evidence from placebo-controlled trials was robust, whereas certainty was lower for indirect estimates, highlighting the need for drug-specific evaluation in clinical practice. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)

A meta-analysis involving 200,068 participants compared [[dulaglutide]] with other GLP-1 receptor agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors, focusing on the incidence of delirium, depression, dementia or coma during treatment. Among all treatments, only high-dose [[dapagliflozin]] (10 mg/day) significantly reduced the occurrence of delirium and depression, particularly in patients with type 2 diabetes. [[dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]] were the only agents associated with cognitive improvement. No significant benefits were observed for dementia or coma across all agents. This analysis highlights agent-specific neuroprotective profiles: SGLT2 inhibitors, especially high-dose [[dapagliflozin]], may mitigate the onset of delirium and depression, while GLP-1 receptor agonists, notably [[dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]], may enhance cognitive function. These findings warrant consideration in selecting antihyperglycemic therapies for individuals at elevated neuropsychiatric risk. (Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine, 2026)
