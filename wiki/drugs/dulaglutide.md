### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis found that cardiovascular and renal benefits of GLP-1-based therapies are outcome-specific. [[Semaglutide]] regimens showed the most consistent reduction in MACE and MI, while [[dulaglutide]] 1.5mg and [[tirzepatide]] 15mg significantly reduced stroke risk. Albuminuria (UACR) showed marked reductions, particularly with [[dulaglutide]] plus [[dapagliflozin]], DPP-4 inhibitors and [[semaglutide]] 1mg. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)

Low-to-moderate certainty evidence indicates that [[semaglutide]] significantly increases risk of nonarteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) relative to non-GLP-1RAs, particularly among patients with diabetes. The study did not find a significant risk for [[dulaglutide]] specifically. (Neurology, 2026)

A meta-analysis involving 200,068 participants compared [[dulaglutide]] with other GLP-1 receptor agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors, focusing on the incidence of delirium, depression, dementia or coma during treatment. Among all treatments, only high-dose [[dapagliflozin]] (10 mg/day) significantly reduced the occurrence of delirium and depression, particularly in patients with type 2 diabetes. [[dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]] were the only agents associated with cognitive improvement. No significant benefits were observed for dementia or coma across all agents. This analysis highlights agent-specific neuroprotective profiles: SGLT2 inhibitors, especially high-dose [[dapagliflozin]], may mitigate the onset of delirium and depression, while GLP-1 receptor agonists, notably [[dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]], may enhance cognitive function. These findings warrant consideration in selecting antihyperglycemic therapies for individuals at elevated neuropsychiatric risk. (Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine, 2026)

A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis involving 80,670 participants compared various antidiabetic agents against placebo for composite renal outcome, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). The primary result showed [[dapagliflozin]] 10 mg showing the greatest efficacy (OR 0.55, 95% CI 0.42-0.72; high-certainty evidence) for composite renal outcomes. Secondary results indicated that [[canagliflozin]] 100-300 mg significantly reduced UACR, whereas [[dapagliflozin]] had no effect. None of the novel antidiabetic agents significantly altered eGFR, and DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. The study concluded: In patients with T2DM and CKD, SGLT2 inhibitors provide the most consistent renal protection, while GLP-1 receptor agonists offer additional but variable benefits. [[dapagliflozin]] showed the greatest efficacy, and [[canagliflozin]] most strongly reduced albuminuria, highlighting meaningful heterogeneity across agents. DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. Overall, evidence from placebo-controlled trials was robust, whereas certainty was lower for indirect estimates, highlighting the need for drug-specific evaluation in clinical practice. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)

A meta-analysis of 25 RCTs involving 2600 patients demonstrated that GLP-1RAs, including [[dulaglutide]], significantly reduced liver fat content by 5.21% and induced histological improvements in steatosis, hepatocellular ballooning, and lobular inflammation in patients with Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) and Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH). Fibrosis improvement was nonsignificant, and no drug-related adverse effects involving the liver were observed. (The Journal of clinical endocrinology and metabolism, 2025)

A meta-analysis found no significant association between GLP-1 RA exposure, including [[dulaglutide]], and the incidence of suicidal behavior in patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus or obesity. (Journal of diabetes, 2025)

A subgroup analysis of the SURPASS-SWITCH trial confirmed that switching from [[dulaglutide]] to [[tirzepatide]] was generally well-tolerated and associated with significant and consistent improvements in HbA1c and weight reductions across all baseline subgroups evaluated. (BMJ open diabetes research & care, 2026)

A meta-analysis of 21 trials found that GLP-1 RAs, including [[dulaglutide]], reduced all-cause death (IRR: 0.88), CV death (IRR: 0.87), and MACE (IRR: 0.87) in high-risk populations, but increased gastrointestinal (+63%) and gallbladder (+26%) disorders. (Journal of the American College of Cardiology, 2025)

This updated systematic review and meta-analysis found a significant association between GLP-1 RA use, including [[dulaglutide]], and increased gastric residual content (GRC). While GLP-1 RA use was linked to higher rates of aborted procedures, no difference in aspiration events was observed between the two groups. Notably, our study identified a protective effect of concurrent colonoscopy on GRC among GLP-1 RA users, while no significant differences were found in GRC risk among different GLP-1 RA agents. (Surgical endoscopy, 2025)

A systematic review and meta-analysis found that [[dulaglutide]] showed a mean weight reduction of 1.93 kg at 56 weeks in pediatric obesity. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)

A post hoc analysis of an RCT found that [[tirzepatide]] demonstrated significant improvements in bodyweight and several metabolic parameters in Japanese participants with type 2 diabetes, with greater improvements observed in metabolic parameters with greater bodyweight loss. [[Dulaglutide]] 0.75 mg was used as a comparator. (Journal of diabetes investigation, 2025)

The SURPASS-SWITCH trial found that switching to [[tirzepatide]] provided additional HbA1c reduction (-1.44% vs -0.67%) and weight loss (-10.5 kg vs -3.6 kg) compared to escalating treatment with [[dulaglutide]] in type 2 diabetes patients. (Annals of internal medicine, 2025)

A systematic review and network meta-analysis found that [[dulaglutide]] showed a significantly higher risk of overall hematologic malignancy (OR = 2.18), especially for lymphoma, while [[tirzepatide]] was linked to a significantly reduced risk (OR = 0.14). (Biomolecules, 2025)

The SURPASS-CVOT trial found that [[tirzepatide]] was noninferior to [[dulaglutide]] for a composite of death from cardiovascular causes, myocardial infarction, or stroke, but more gastrointestinal adverse events were observed in the [[tirzepatide]] group. (The New England journal of medicine, 2025)

A meta-analysis of 38 phase 3 or 4 placebo-controlled RCTs involving 16,660 individuals with type 2 diabetes across various incretin mimetics (including [[dulaglutide]]) found that while the magnitude of efficacy for HbA1c and body weight reduction varied widely, larger therapeutic efficacy was not systematically associated with higher gastrointestinal adverse event or drug discontinuation rates, suggesting better tolerability of more effective agents. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)

An RCT with 275 adults with type 2 diabetes found that [[retatrutide]] doses of ≥4 mg significantly reduced overall appetite, hunger, and prospective food consumption compared to placebo. Specifically, [[retatrutide]] 8 and 12 mg improved Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition compared to placebo, and greater weight reduction was associated with decreased perceived hunger and disinhibition and increased dietary restraint. [[dulaglutide]] 1.5 mg was used as a comparator. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)

In an RCT involving 189 participants with type 2 diabetes, [[retatrutide]] 8 mg (pooled) showed a 26.1% reduction in total fat mass from baseline, significantly outperforming [[dulaglutide]] (2.6% reduction) and placebo. The study concluded that [[retatrutide]] significantly improved total body fat mass reduction compared with placebo and [[dulaglutide]], with a greater proportion of lean mass not being lost despite overall increased weight loss. (The lancet. Diabetes & endocrinology, 2025)

A meta-analysis of 62 studies (66,232 patients) on glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs), including [[dulaglutide]], showed a significantly increased risk of pancreatitis (RR: 1.44), though this risk was not significant when stratified by background medications. Overall, no significant association was found between GLP-1 RA use and pancreatic cancer, although a significant increase in risk was observed with background medications (RR: 1.85), which was deemed "likely minimal." (Endocrinology, diabetes & metabolism, 2025)
