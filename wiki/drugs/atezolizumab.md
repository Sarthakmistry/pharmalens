### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis published in The Cochrane database of systematic reviews in 2026, involving 4341 participants, investigated PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy compared with chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery. The primary outcome was pathological complete response, event-free survival (EFS), and overall survival (OS). The primary result was: PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably increase pathological complete response rate (RR 1.47, 95% CI 1.15 to 1.86; 6 studies, 1564 participants); improve EFS (HR 0.64, 95% CI 0.52 to 0.79; 4 studies, 1789 participants); and probably improve OS (HR 0.56, 95% CI 0.34 to 0.93; 3 studies, 1681 participants) compared with chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery. Secondary results included: PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably increase immune-related SAEs (OR 1.75, 95% CI 1.15 to 2.67; 5 studies, 2016 participants) compared with chemotherapy alone after breast cancer surgery. There may be little or no difference between PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy and chemotherapy alone in HRQoL (MD -1.49, 95% CI -3.88 to 0.91; 2 studies, 1395 participants) before breast cancer surgery. PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably have little or no effect on any adverse events (OR 0.26, 95% CI -0.05 to 1.24; 3 studies, 1781 participants) and treatment-related deaths (RD 0.2%, 95% CI -0.4% to 0.8%; 4 studies, 1761 participants) before breast cancer surgery. There may be little or no difference between PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy and chemotherapy alone in EFS (HR 1.11, 95% CI 0.87 to 1.42; 1 study, 2199 participants) and OS (HR 1.23, 95% CI 0.87 to 1.73; 1 study, 2199 participants) after breast cancer surgery. PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably increase immune-related SAEs (OR 1.81, 95% CI 1.47 to 2.24; 1 study, 2177 participants) compared to chemotherapy alone after breast cancer surgery. The addition of PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors to chemotherapy probably increases immune-related serious adverse events, both before and after breast cancer surgery. Conclusions: Combining PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy compared to chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery improves pathological response, EFS, and OS in early TNBC. In contrast, the combination of PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy after breast cancer surgery may have little to no effect on EFS and OS in early-stage TNBC when compared with chemotherapy alone. The addition of PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors probably increases immune-related SAEs.
A multicentre, single-arm, phase 2 trial (NCT04776447) published in Nature communications in 2025, involving 38 patients with non-resectable stage IIIA-IIIC NSCLC, investigated ChIO induction before CT-RT and IO maintenance with [[atezolizumab]]. The primary outcome was 12-month progression-free survival (PFS). PFS was 68.4% (95% CI, 51.1-80.7) at 12 months (statistically significant compared to null hypothesis of 55%). Secondary results included PFS was 50.0% at 24 months, OS was 86.8% at 12 months and 60.5% at 24 months, After induction, 18 (47.4%) had partial response, 14 (36.8%) stable disease, and 5 (13.2%) progressive disease, and Overall, 18 patients had disease progression: 8 local (44.4%) and 10 distant (55.6%). Grade ≥3 TRAEs occurred in 11 (28.9%) during induction, 10 (26.3%) during CT-RT, and 2 (5.3%) during maintenance. ChIO induction before CT-RT and IO maintenance showed activity and safety, warranting confirmation in larger studies.
A meta-analysis published in Frontiers in immunology in 2026, involving 13572 patients, compared various ICI-based combinations against sorafenib for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS) in advanced hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). In the overall population, regimens with significant OS advantage over sorafenib included sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar (HR = 0.57, 95% CrI 0.43-0.75), camrelizumab plus rivoceranib (HR = 0.62, 0.48-0.79), and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab (HR = 0.66, 0.51-0.84). For PFS, top-ranked combinations were camrelizumab plus rivoceranib (HR = 0.52, 0.41-0.66), anlotinib plus penpulimab (HR = 0.53, 0.41-0.68), lenvatinib plus [[pembrolizumab]] (HR = 0.55, 0.44-0.68), and sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar (HR = 0.56, 0.45-0.69). ORR was highest with lenvatinib plus [[pembrolizumab]] (RR = 8.00, 4.98-12.86). In HBV-related HCC, sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab led OS rankings, with PFS favoring cabozantinib plus [[atezolizumab]] and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab. In HCV-related HCC, only [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab conferred a significant OS benefit (HR = 0.43, 0.25-0.73), while PFS superiority was observed only for cabozantinib plus [[atezolizumab]] (HR = 0.73, 0.54-0.99). In non-viral HCC, the STRIDE regimen (single priming dose tremelimumab plus [[durvalumab]]) was the only regimen to significantly improve OS (HR = 0.75, 0.59-0.96). Regarding safety, tislelizumab (RR = 0.42, 0.33-0.52) and [[nivolumab]] (RR = 0.45, 0.36-0.56) were associated with the lowest incidence of AEs≥3. For first-line therapy in advanced HCC, ICI-based combinations with anti-VEGF/anti-angiogenic agents generally outperform sorafenib, with discernible etiology-specific optima: HBV-related HCC favors sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar or atezolizumab plus bevacizumab; HCV-related HCC favors atezolizumab plus bevacizumab; and in non-viral disease, STRIDE demonstrates a unique OS advantage. This etiology-stratified evidence framework may guide individualized first-line decision-making, pending confirmation in head-to-head trials.
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) published in Annals of oncology : official journal of the European Society of Medical Oncology in 2024, involving 400 patients, investigated [[atezolizumab]] in combination with [[bevacizumab]] and carboplatin-pemetrexed (ABC) for advanced pleural mesothelioma, compared against bevacizumab and carboplatin-pemetrexed chemotherapy (BC). The primary outcome was overall survival (OS) in all randomised patients. The primary result was: median OS was 20.5 months for ABC versus 18.1 months for BC (HR 0.84, 95% CI 0.66-1.06, P = 0.14). Secondary results included: Median PFS was significantly longer for ABC than for BC (9.2 versus 7.6 months) (HR 0.72, 95% CI 0.59-0.89, P = 0.0021). Grade ≥3 treatment-related AEs were reported in 55% of patients in ABC and 47% in BC; QoL was maintained with ABC with no clinically meaningful differences from BC. The significant benefit in median PFS for ABC found in this study translated into a numerical but not significant increase in median OS. Thus, the primary endpoint was not met. In the pre-specified analysis by histology, superior OS and PFS were found for ABC in non-epithelioid cases.

A meta-analysis published in Asian Pacific journal of cancer prevention : APJCP in 2026, involving Eleven studies, compared atezolizumab plus bevacizumab against competitor drug for Overall survival (OS). The network meta-analysis showed no significant OS differences between atezolizumab plus bevacizumab and lenvatinib (HR: 0.98; 95% CI: 0.24-4.10) or sorafenib (HR: 1.4; 95% CI: 0.21-9.87). Furthermore, there was no significant difference in OS between lenvatinib and sorafenib (HR: 1.41; 95% CI: 0.38-5.14). Based on the SUCRA plot in this meta-analysis, atezolizumab plus bevacizumab showed the highest probability of being ranked first among the three therapies. Lenvatinib had the highest probability of being ranked second, while sorafenib was more likely to be ranked third. Atezolizumab plus bevacizumab, lenvatinib, and sorafenib demonstrated similar therapeutic efficacy based on overall survival. Although the hazard ratios (HRs) were not statistically significant, the SUCRA ranking suggested a clinical trend favoring atezolizumab plus bevacizumab.
A meta-analysis published in Cancer medicine in 2026, involving 5954 patients, compared various antiangiogenic combinations against chemotherapy for progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), objective response rate (ORR), and incidence of grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) in advanced driver-negative non-small cell lung cancer. Sintilimab + anlotinib achieved the greatest progression-free survival (PFS) benefit (HR = 0.39, 95% CI 0.23-0.67) and the lowest incidence of grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) (RR = 0.57, 95% CI 0.32-0.95). Recombinant human endostatin (Endostar) + chemotherapy provided the largest overall survival (OS) benefit (HR = 0.46, 95% CI 0.36-0.58). The triplet regimen of [[atezolizumab]], [[bevacizumab]], and chemotherapy yielded the largest improvement in objective response rate (ORR) (OR = 1.90, 95% CI 1.40-2.60). Across most subgroup analyses, regimens combining immunotherapy, an antiangiogenic agent, and chemotherapy conferred the greatest PFS and OS benefits. Sintilimab + anlotinib achieved the lowest incidence of grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) (RR = 0.57, 95% CI 0.32-0.95). This network meta-analysis demonstrates that antiangiogenic combinations improve outcomes in driver-negative advanced NSCLC. Endostar + chemotherapy offers the greatest OS benefit, atezolizumab-bevacizumab-chemotherapy improves ORR, and sintilimab-anlotinib provides superior PFS with lower toxicity. Treatment should be tailored based on clinical factors, with further validation in multiethnic trials.
An RCT published in Journal for immunotherapy of cancer in 2026, involving 66 patients, investigated the combination of FAP-IL2v plus [[atezolizumab]] with or without [[bevacizumab]] for unresectable, metastatic renal cell carcinoma (mRCC) with clear cell and/or sarcomatoid features. The study design was RCT, with doublet therapy (FAP-IL2v + [[atezolizumab]]) as a comparator. The primary outcome was recommended FAP-IL2v dose and antitumor activity. The maximum tolerated dose was not reached, with a recommended FAP-IL2v dose of 10 mg. Objective response rates (ORRs) were 25% for the doublet and 47% for the triplet. Secondary results included: Median progression-free survival was 6.3 and 18.3 months, respectively. Safety profiles were consistent with the individual drugs, including expected interleukin-2 (IL-2) class-specific adverse events (AEs). Expansion and activation of NK and T cells, but not regulatory T cells, was observed in peripheral blood, leading to increased tumor infiltration and inflammation in paired biopsies. The addition of [[bevacizumab]] led to a reduced angiogenesis signature score and reduced vessel density. Two deaths were recorded caused by AEs related to study treatment (acute kidney injury, n=1; pancytopenia, n=1). Conclusions: The combination of FAP-IL2v plus atezolizumab with or without bevacizumab was consistent with the known safety profile of the individual drugs. The maximum tolerated dose was not reached, with a recommended FAP-IL2v dose of 10 mg. ORR was higher among patients receiving the triplet therapy compared with the doublet. Pharmacodynamic results were consistent with the mechanism of action of IL-2, supporting further research in this field.
A Clinical Trial, Phase II study published in Annals of oncology : official journal of the European Society for Medical Oncology in 2026, involving 192 patients, investigated [[atezolizumab]] for muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC). An industry-sponsored RCT published in The Journal of clinical investigation in 2026, involving 305 treatment-naive clear-cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) patients, investigated the relationship between molecular subgroups and metastatic tropism, and prediction of response to therapy, with sunitinib as a comparator. Angiogenic tumors (clusters 1 and 2) had a higher rate of pancreatic (21% vs. 6.9%; P = 0.002) and lower absolute number of lymph node (2.5 vs. 4.2; P = 0.006) metastases. Patients with pancreatic metastases receiving sunitinib had higher odds of overall response (OR, 7.13; 95% CI, 1.81-28.07; P = 0.0049) and longer progression-free survival than those without pancreatic metastases (P = 0.02). ccRCC metastatic tropism relates to molecular clusters that predict response to therapy for tumors that metastasize to the pancreas.

### Competitive position
The recent clinical findings from International journal of radiation oncology, biology, physics (2025) on SBRT and [[atezolizumab]] in metastatic cervical cancer, showing well-tolerated combination therapy and responses in PD-L1 negative tumors, suggest a potential for improved response rates in this indication. This could strengthen [[atezolizumab]]'s competitive position by offering a new therapeutic strategy, particularly for patients with PD-L1 negative tumors who might have limited options. The recent clinical trial results published in Nature communications (2026) for [[atezolizumab]] in combination with LOAd703 for metastatic malignant melanoma, showing promising efficacy and tolerability in a refractory patient population, could expand its utility and strengthen its competitive position in difficult-to-treat melanoma cases. [[Atezolizumab]], as a PD-L1 inhibitor, competes with other checkpoint inhibitors like [[pembrolizumab]] (Keytruda) and [[nivolumab]] (Opdivo) across various oncology indications, including NSCLC and breast cancer. Its combination therapies, such as with [[bevacizumab]], aim to differentiate it by targeting specific patient populations or disease subtypes where synergistic effects are observed. For instance, in hepatocellular carcinoma, the [[atezolizumab]]-[[bevacizumab]] combination has shown a favorable balance between QoL preservation and overall survival. The recent findings from a study published in Journal of hepatology (2025) demonstrating lenvatinib's efficacy as a second-line treatment for HCC progressing on atezo-bev further solidifies the treatment pathway in HCC, potentially reinforcing atezo-bev's role as a first-line option by providing a clear subsequent therapy. The clinical trial results published in Signal transduction and targeted therapy (2025) further support the safety and feasibility of TACE combined with [[atezolizumab]] and [[bevacizumab]] for intermediate-stage HCC, reinforcing its potential in this area. However, a recent study in resectable stage III melanoma showed that [[atezolizumab]] plus tiragolumab had a lower pathological response rate compared to [[nivolumab]] plus ipilimumab, suggesting a competitive disadvantage for this specific combination in this indication. A recent systematic review and network meta-analysis in Frontiers in immunology (2025) provided a comprehensive comparison of various immune checkpoint inhibitors in advanced NSCLC, highlighting different agents for optimal OS and PFS benefits based on patient characteristics and PD-L1 expression, further defining the competitive landscape for drugs like [[atezolizumab]]. The failure of the ALBAN trial to show an EFS benefit for [[atezolizumab]] in high-risk NMIBC, especially when contrasted with positive results from another PD-(L)1 agent in a similar context, suggests that its efficacy may be more context- and agent-specific, potentially impacting its competitive standing in bladder cancer. The positive results from the IMvigor011 trial, showing significantly longer disease-free and overall survival with ctDNA-guided adjuvant atezolizumab in muscle-invasive bladder cancer, strengthen its competitive position in this specific oncology area. Furthermore, a meta-analysis in Clinical genitourinary cancer (2025) on ICIs for BCG-unresponsive NMIBC, while showing encouraging effectiveness, noted that the [[atezolizumab]] group had a greater incidence of serious adverse events (21%) compared to the [[pembrolizumab]] group (11%), indicating a potential safety differentiation point for competitors in this indication. The ongoing research into new indications and combination regimens, such as in cervical cancer, seeks to expand its market share and clinical utility against established and emerging treatments. The recent positive Phase 3 results for zanzalintinib-[[atezolizumab]] in metastatic colorectal cancer, demonstrating a significant overall survival benefit, position [[atezolizumab]] as a potential chemotherapy-free treatment option in a heavily pretreated patient population, enhancing its competitive standing in the oncology-crc indication. A recent RCT published in Frontiers in immunology in 2025 identified prognostic biomarkers (mGPS, ACR, AIR) for overall survival in hepatocellular carcinoma patients, which could aid in patient stratification and management for existing treatments like [[atezolizumab]] and sorafenib, though it does not directly alter the drugs' efficacy or competitive standing. The identification of higher TILs or ECs in the cancer area as biomarkers for favorable response to subsequent ICI-based treatment in EGFR-mutated NSCLC, as shown in a 2025 RCT in Journal for immunotherapy of cancer, could help refine patient selection for [[atezolizumab]] and improve its competitive positioning in this specific patient population.
