### Management sentiment
Management sentiment is generally bullish for trastuzumab deruxtecan, driven by significant efficacy findings across various breast cancer subtypes and gastric cancer. Multiple studies highlight its superior progression-free and overall survival benefits compared to standard chemotherapy, leading to its confirmation as a standard of care in HER2-low metastatic breast cancer. While safety concerns, particularly interstitial lung disease and other adverse events, are noted, they are often described as manageable, and preliminary clinical activity in combination therapies further supports a positive outlook.

### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis (684 patients) on T-DXd safety in breast cancer brain metastases (BCBM) found a pooled incidence of all-grade ILD/pneumonitis of 10% (grade ≥3 at 2%), leading to treatment discontinuation in 11% of patients, though no fatal ILD events were reported. Overall, 97% of patients experienced any-grade treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs), with 48% experiencing grade ≥3 TEAEs, highlighting the need for vigilant monitoring.

A Phase Ib study (DESTINY-Breast08, 138 patients) investigating T-DXd in combination with capecitabine, durvalumab + paclitaxel, capivasertib, anastrozole, or fulvestrant in HER2-low metastatic breast cancer demonstrated preliminary clinical activity, with objective response rates ranging from 40% to 71.4% across different combinations. Safety profiles were generally consistent with known individual drug profiles.

A systematic review (13 studies, 1566 NSCLC patients) on ADC monotherapy safety indicated a high pooled incidence of all-grade treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) at 93.67%, with grade ≥3 TRAEs in 38.74%. Fatal TRAEs were mainly respiratory, and T-DXd was more likely to cause grade ≥3 pneumonitis or interstitial lung disease (ILD).

A systematic review and meta-analysis (7 studies, 2995 patients) confirmed that T-DXd significantly prolonged median progression-free survival (HR=0.43) and median overall survival (HR=0.72) compared to control in breast cancer patients, alongside a significantly higher objective remission rate. However, it also increased the incidence of adverse events such as anemia, nausea, vomiting, constipation, and interstitial lung disease.

A meta-analysis (9538 patients) evaluating cardiotoxic effects (LVEF decrease) in ERBB2-positive advanced breast cancer found that trastuzumab emtansine was associated with the lowest incidence (0.94%), while T-DXd (4.20%), trastuzumab plus chemotherapy (4.85%), and trastuzumab plus pertuzumab plus chemotherapy (5.52%) had similar, higher incidences.

In HER2-positive early breast cancer, a randomized controlled trial (927 patients) showed that neoadjuvant T-DXd-THP (trastuzumab deruxtecan, paclitaxel, trastuzumab, pertuzumab) demonstrated a statistically significant and clinically meaningful pathological complete response (pCR) benefit (67.3% vs 56.3%; difference 11.2%; P=0.003) and improved safety compared to dose-dense doxorubicin + cyclophosphamide (ddAC) followed by THP.

Extended follow-up data from DESTINY-Breast04 (NCT03734029) demonstrated that T-DXd significantly improved overall survival (median 22.9 months vs 16.8 months; HR 0.69) and progression-free survival compared with treatment of physician's choice of chemotherapy (TPC) in HER2-low metastatic breast cancer, confirming T-DXd as a standard of care.

A randomized controlled trial (111 patients) in ERBB2-positive metastatic breast cancer patients receiving T-DXd suggested that electronic symptom and vital sign tracking may help maintain quality of life (QoL) or prevent its deterioration, with a higher mean change in global health status score at week 24 (mean difference, 8.0) in the monitoring group.

The FDA approved T-DXd for adult patients with hormone receptor-positive, unresectable or metastatic HER2-low or HER2-ultralow breast cancer based on the DESTINY-Breast06 trial, which showed a statistically significant improvement in progression-free survival (13.2 months vs 8.1 months; HR 0.62) compared to investigator's choice chemotherapy. This marks the first indication specifying HER2-ultralow expression.

A systematic review and meta-analysis (35 studies, 48 distinct cohorts) on T-DXd safety in solid tumors concluded that T-DXd has a safety and tolerability profile less favorable than classical chemotherapy, with all-grade adverse events (AEs) occurring in 97.2% of patients and grade ≥3 AEs in 54.9%. The pooled incidence of interstitial lung disease (ILD) was 13.2%, with treatment-related deaths reported in 5% of patients.

In HER2-positive metastatic gastric cancer or gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma, a randomized controlled trial (494 patients) showed that T-DXd led to significantly longer overall survival (median 14.7 months vs 11.4 months; HR 0.70; P=0.004) and progression-free survival compared to ramucirumab plus paclitaxel. The objective response rate was 44.3% for T-DXd vs 29.1% for the comparator, though interstitial lung disease or pneumonitis occurred in 13.9% of T-DXd patients.

A randomized controlled trial (494 patients) comparing T-DXd with physician's choice of chemotherapy in previously treated HER2-low metastatic breast cancer demonstrated that T-DXd maintained global health status/quality of life (GHS/QoL) scores despite a longer treatment course and delayed the median time to definitive deterioration (TDD) for GHS/QoL (11.4 vs 7.5 months; HR, 0.69).

A randomized controlled trial (58 patients) investigating optimal antiemetic therapy for Japanese patients with gastric cancer undergoing T-DXd treatment found that neither doublet nor triplet antiemetic regimens met the primary endpoint for complete response to emetic events during cycle 1, suggesting a need for further research in supportive care.

A meta-analysis (103 trials) using a Clinical Utility Index (CUI) successfully predicted the approved T-DXd dose (5.4 mg/kg for breast cancer), demonstrating CUI improvement and suggesting that this approach can optimize dosing and save costs.

A meta-analysis (9 trials, 5753 patients) on Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) in metastatic breast cancer highlighted that ADCs, including T-DXd, significantly delayed clinical deterioration of global health status and improved various aspects of quality of life. However, it also noted common adverse events for T-DXd, such as nausea, anemia, interstitial lung disease, and left ventricular dysfunction.

### Competitive position
Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd) is a key player in the ADC landscape, particularly in breast cancer. Comparative meta-analyses show T-DXd demonstrating favorable outcomes across progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), and objective response rate (ORR) compared to other ADCs like sacituzumab govitecan (SG) and datopotamab deruxtecan (Dato-DXd), especially in earlier lines of treatment for pretreated HR+/HER2-low metastatic breast cancer. While Dato-DXd showed PFS benefit, it did not achieve OS benefit, and its safety profile was more favorable than T-DXd or SG. In terms of cardiotoxicity, T-DXd showed a higher incidence of LVEF decrease compared to trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1), but similar to other trastuzumab-based regimens. The competitive landscape also includes traditional chemotherapies, against which T-DXd has shown superior efficacy but sometimes a less favorable safety profile, necessitating careful management of adverse events.
