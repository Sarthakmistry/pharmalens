### Management sentiment
Management sentiment is bullish due to the beneficial effects of semaglutide 2.4 mg versus placebo, supporting its use in an adult Chinese population with overweight or obesity. Semaglutide 2.4mg use as in SELECT is cost-effective at list price, using a $150,000/QALY willingness-to-pay threshold. The positive results from the cagrilintide-semaglutide combination therapy, showing significantly lower body weight and higher glycemic target achievement compared to placebo, further contribute to this bullish outlook. Additionally, cagrilintide-semaglutide provided significant and clinically relevant body-weight reductions in adults with overweight or obesity, as compared with placebo. Furthermore, CagriSema presents clinically relevant reductions in BP across a wide range of participant subgroups. Recent findings also highlight semaglutide's efficacy in MASH treatment, significant blood pressure reduction, weight management post-bariatric surgery, and positive impact on conditions like PCOS and obesity-related knee osteoarthritis. While comparative studies with tirzepatide show competitive pressure in weight and HbA1c reduction, semaglutide's broad benefits and cost-effectiveness in T2D maintain a strong bullish outlook, reinforced by numerous recent trial completions across various indications.

### Clinical evidence
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis involving 80,670 participants compared various treatments against placebo for composite renal outcome, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). The primary result showed dapagliflozin 10 mg showing the greatest efficacy (OR 0.55, 95% CI 0.42-0.72; high-certainty evidence) for composite renal outcomes. Secondary results indicated that [[canagliflozin]] 100-300 mg significantly reduced UACR, whereas [[dapagliflozin]] had no effect. None of the novel antidiabetic agents significantly altered eGFR, and DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. In patients with T2DM and CKD, SGLT2 inhibitors provide the most consistent renal protection, while GLP-1 receptor agonists offer additional but variable benefits. Dapagliflozin showed the greatest efficacy, and canagliflozin most strongly reduced albuminuria, highlighting meaningful heterogeneity across agents. DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. Overall, evidence from placebo-controlled trials was robust, whereas certainty was lower for indirect estimates, highlighting the need for drug-specific evaluation in clinical practice. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)
A Network Meta-Analysis involving 1667 patients compared various SGLT2 inhibitors and incretin-based agents against placebo for fibrosis improvement (≥1 stage) without worsening steatohepatitis. All active treatments, including [[dapagliflozin]] 10 mg, Survodutide (2.4 mg/wk, 4.8-6 mg/wk), [[tirzepatide]] (5 mg/wk, 10-15 mg/wk), and [[semaglutide]] (0.7-1.4 mg/wk, 2.4 or 2.8 mg/wk), improved fibrosis versus placebo (I2 = 0%). For MASH resolution, dose-dependent effects led to significant heterogeneity (I2 = 73%), with lower-dose Semaglutide demonstrating no benefits and Dapagliflozin showing benefits in the F2-F3 subgroup only on sensitivity analysis. Survodutide exhibited the highest ranking (SUCRA = 0.822-0.849), followed by Tirzepatide (SUCRA = 0.622-0.681) and higher-dose Semaglutide (SUCRA = 0.327) for MASH resolution. Meta-regression using data from 16 interventions, including placebo arms, showed that weight loss significantly explained heterogeneity in treatment effects on fibrosis improvement (R2 = 54.26%) and MASH resolution (R2 = 78.16%). No safety concerns were reported. SGLT2 inhibitor and incretin-based agents improved fibrosis in MASH, with weight loss being a significant mediator. Targeting multiple incretin pathways, especially involving glucagon receptors, may offer greater MASH resolution. Dose-dependent effects were more prominent for MASH resolution than fibrosis improvement, indicating potential weight-loss-independent anti-fibrotic pathways. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)
A meta-analysis involving 3173 subjects compared various treatments to placebo for changes in biopsy MASLD Activity Scores (MAS). Steatohepatitis scores (MAS) improved with treatment versus placebo by mean difference (md) = -1.27 ± 0.16 SD Units, p < 0.001. MAS sub scores improved for steatosis, lobar inflammation, and ballooning for [[dapagliflozin]], [[semaglutide]], and pioglitazone (all p < 0.002). Fibrosis Grades improved compared to placebo (md = -0.352 ± 0.03 Units, p < 0.001). Relative rates (rr) of RSw/oF and RFw/oS were found with resmetirom, [[semaglutide]], [[tirzepatide]], and [[dapagliflozin]] (all p < 0.015). Changes in RSw/oF and RFw/oS inversely correlated with the baseline levels of Fibrosis Grades (p = 0.025, and p = 0.076 respectively), with greater improvements of both at lower Fibrosis Grades. Clinically available medications are beneficial in reversing MASH. Improvements in RSw/oF and RFw/oS were greater at earlier stages of fibrosis. Future analyses of drug effects should include assessments adjusted for baseline study characteristics of Fibrosis Grades and may evaluate whether preventive therapy will have long term benefits if started at earlier stages of MASLD. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2026)
A meta-analysis of 4 randomized controlled trials (n = 4419 patients) compared CagriSema to [[semaglutide]] monotherapy or placebo. The primary outcome was percent weight loss. CagriSema significantly reduced percent weight loss (Cohen's d: -1.38; 95% CI: -1.84 to -0.91; I² = 94.8%). Secondary results included absolute weight loss (MD: -11 kg), waist circumference (MD: -9.41 cm), and systolic blood pressure (MD: -7.06 mmHg). Gastrointestinal adverse events were more frequent (RR: 1.32). In conclusion, CagriSema achieves greater weight loss than semaglutide or placebo but increases gastrointestinal adverse events, warranting careful tolerability monitoring and longer-term data. This study was published in The American journal of cardiology in 2026.
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis involving 12 articles investigated the effect of GLP-1RAs, including semaglutide, on heart rate (HR) in non-diabetic individuals with overweight or obesity. GLP-1RAs were associated with an increase in HR. Pairwise meta-analysis showed significant HR increase compared with placebo for semaglutide (MD 3.35). Network meta-analysis revealed orforglipron 36 mg was associated with the most pronounced HR increase (MD 9.29), while tirzepatide 5 mg was associated with the least (MD 0.52). The study concluded that GLP-1RAs were associated with an increasing of HR in patients with overweight or obesity. (European journal of medical research, 2026)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 3417 participants compared CagriSema versus placebo. The primary outcome was Changes from baseline to week 68 in blood pressure (BP). Changes from baseline to week 68 in blood pressure (BP) were greater with CagriSema versus placebo (systolic BP: -10.9 versus -2.8 mm Hg; diastolic BP: -5.4 versus -1.7 mm Hg, respectively). The proportion of participants reaching BP targets at week 68 was 63.0% and 32.0% for CagriSema and placebo, respectively. The proportion of participants with resistant hypertension at baseline (n=167) that reached BP targets at week 68 was 42.0% and 29.3% for CagriSema and placebo, respectively (odds ratio, 1.7 [95% CI, 0.7-4.4]). Among participants who used antihypertensive medication during the study, 39.6% in the CagriSema group decreased or stopped treatment from week 0 to week 68 versus 18.8% with placebo. CagriSema presents clinically relevant reductions in BP across a wide range of participant subgroups, including those with resistant hypertension. This industry-sponsored study was published in Hypertension (Dallas, Tex. : 1979) in 2026.
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis involving 3311 participants compared various therapies for MASH. The primary outcome was the absolute change in MRI-PDFF. For MRI-PDFF decline at 24 weeks, aldafermin (SUCRA: 83.65), pegozafermin (SUCRA: 83.46), and pioglitazone (SUCRA: 71.67) were ranked the most effective interventions. At 24 weeks, efinopegdutide (SUCRA: 67.02), [[semaglutide]] + firsocostat (SUCRA: 62.43), and pegbelfermin (SUCRA: 61.68) were ranked the most effective interventions for achieving a ≥30% decline in MRI-PDFF. This study provides an updated, relative rank-order efficacy of therapies for MASH in reducing hepatic fat. A meta-analysis of 25 RCTs involving 2600 patients using GLP-1RAs, including semaglutide, was conducted to assess their impact on MASLD and MASH. GLP-1RAs treatment for a median of 24 weeks demonstrated a significant reduction in liver fat content (LFC) by 5.21%. GLP-1RAs induced significant histological improvements in steatosis, hepatocellular ballooning, and lobular inflammation but nonsignificantly improved fibrosis. A meta-analysis of 29,506 patients compared retatrutide, dual agonists, and GLP-1RAs (including semaglutide) for weight loss. Semaglutide significantly reduced weight after bariatric surgery (WMD: -15.730, 95% CI: -22.140, -9.320, p < 0.001). The current data encourages the use of semaglutide for managing weight regain or inadequate weight loss post-bariatric surgery. (Obesity surgery, 2025)
A meta-analysis of 45 articles (119 comparisons) from 2019 onward assessed the cost-effectiveness of semaglutide against other glucose-lowering medications for type 2 diabetes. Semaglutide was dominant/cost-effective in 73.9% of all comparisons. Cost-effectiveness varied by study sponsor type (100% in Novo Nordisk-sponsored studies vs. 50.0% in non-industry sponsored studies). Semaglutide was more cost-effective in high-income countries and in studies with a broader perspective, longer horizon, and lower discount rates. (Diabetes care, 2025)
A meta-analysis investigated the effects of obesity medications, including GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide, on weight, BMI, and total weight loss post-bariatric surgery. GLP-1 receptor agonists reduced weight by 8.38 kg (95% CI: -9.68 to -7.08) and BMI by 3.76 kg/m² (95% CI: -4.50 to -3.01). The overall %TWL for GLP-1 receptor agonists was 9.94% (95% CI: 8.34 to 11.53). Semaglutide showed a significantly greater effect on %TWL compared to liraglutide. The study concluded that obesity medications may be an effective adjunctive therapy to maintain weight loss post-bariatric surgery. (International journal of obesity (2005), 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 101 patients with T2D and CVD investigated the effect of semaglutide on atherosclerotic inflammation in carotid arteries using PET-MRI. No significant treatment differences were observed between semaglutide and placebo for change in plaque inflammation at week 26. The lack of detected effect is likely due to minimal baseline inflammation, and does not exclude an effect of semaglutide on inflammation seen in previous preclinical and clinical studies. This industry-sponsored study explored the feasibility of following plaque inflammation with PET-MRI. (American heart journal, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 247 participants compared cotadutide (a dual GLP-1/glucagon receptor agonist) to 1mg semaglutide or placebo for chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes. A PK/PD model showed significant relationships between cotadutide exposure and reductions in UACR, UALB, and body weight. After 26 weeks, 600 µg cotadutide treatment led to model-predicted relative changes from placebo of -45.6% in UACR, -47.2% in UALB, and -5.3% in body weight. This industry-sponsored study provides insights into cotadutide's potential in diabetic kidney disease. (British journal of clinical pharmacology, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 30 women with obesity and PCOS investigated semaglutide's effect on taste recognition and brain activity. Semaglutide improved overall taste recognition score from 11.9 ± 1.9 points to 14.4 ± 1.0 points, with an estimated treatment difference of 2.5 points (95% CI, 1.7-3.3). Semaglutide altered RNA expression in the tongue related to taste signaling and renewal of taste buds, and modified brain activity in response to sweet and savory food cues and sweet solution. (The Journal of clinical endocrinology and metabolism, 2025)
A meta-analysis of 9 RCTs compared metformin, Metformin-Phosphatidylcholine (MFPH), semaglutide, and liraglutide against usual care for obesity-related knee osteoarthritis (KOA) pain. Metformin demonstrated the largest effect size for pain relief (MD -1.13, 95% CI -1.48, -0.78). Semaglutide showed pain relief with a mean difference of -0.90 (95% CI -1.48, -0.32) and a favorable safety profile (0.21, 95% CI -0.46, 0.88). Metformin ranked highest for efficacy (SUCRA: 86.8%), while usual care ranked highest for safety (SUCRA: 75.7%). Semaglutide offers a balanced efficacy and safety profile. (Aging clinical and experimental research, 2025)
A comparative study using indirect treatment comparison (Bucher method) compared tirzepatide 10 and 15 mg against semaglutide 2.4 mg for obesity or overweight and type 2 diabetes. Tirzepatide 10 and 15 mg showed significantly greater mean percent weight reductions than semaglutide (10 mg: 2.57%; 15 mg: 4.79%, p < 0.01). Both tirzepatide doses were associated with significantly greater reductions in HbA1c (%) levels than semaglutide (10 mg: 0.47%; 15 mg: 0.56%, p < 0.001). Tirzepatide 15 mg had significantly higher odds of achieving ≥5% weight reduction (OR 1.76, p = 0.035). This industry-sponsored analysis suggested greater reductions in bodyweight and HbA1c with tirzepatide. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 800 patients with biopsy-defined MASH and fibrosis stage 2 or 3 investigated semaglutide 2.4 mg once weekly. Resolution of steatohepatitis without worsening of fibrosis occurred in 62.9% of the semaglutide group versus 34.3% of the placebo group (P<0.001). Reduction in liver fibrosis without worsening of steatohepatitis occurred in 36.8% of the semaglutide group versus 22.4% of the placebo group (P<0.001). The mean change in body weight was -10.5% with semaglutide and -2.0% with placebo (P<0.001). Gastrointestinal adverse events were more common in the semaglutide group. This industry-sponsored study concluded that semaglutide improved liver histologic results in MASH patients. (The New England journal of medicine, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) evaluated semaglutide's effect on hepatic steatosis in type 2 diabetes patients. Liver fat was quantified using unenhanced cardiac CT scans. After 12 months, average liver attenuation improved by 4.4 HU in the semaglutide group compared to placebo (p = 0.002), after adjusting for confounders. The semaglutide group showed an improvement of 1.4 ± 9.0 mean HU, while the placebo group showed a worsening of 1.9 ± 9.5 mean HU. Treatment with semaglutide resulted in a significant improvement in fatty liver reduction when compared to placebo. (Nutrition, metabolism, and cardiovascular diseases : NMCD, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 245 individuals with type 2 diabetes and overweight or obesity investigated higher doses of semaglutide (up to 16 mg/week). Higher doses (16 mg vs 2 mg) resulted in an additional -3.4 kg weight loss (P = 0.011) and a modest additional HbA1c reduction of -0.3 percentage points (P = 0.245). Treatment-emergent adverse events and discontinuations due to AEs were more frequent in the 8 mg and 16 mg semaglutide groups, primarily gastrointestinal. No severe hypoglycemic episodes were reported. This industry-sponsored study suggests higher doses provide modest additional benefits at the expense of more AEs. (Diabetes care, 2025)
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis compared the effect of different drug-dose combinations of GLP-1RAs on systolic blood pressure (SBP) in patients with obesity. GLP-1RAs significantly lowered SBP for all patients (MD = -3.14 [-3.60; -2.68]). SBP reduction was significantly greater for patients without diabetes (-3.80 [-4.24; -3.37]) compared to patients with diabetes (-2.13 [-3.27; -1.00]). The change in SBP appears to be related to the amount of weight loss. (Clinical obesity, 2025)
A meta-analysis of 38 phase 3 or 4 placebo-controlled RCTs of incretin mimetics, including semaglutide, in 16,660 individuals with type 2 diabetes. HbA1c reductions ranged from -0.63% (lixisenatide) to -1.79% (tirzepatide), and weight reductions from -0.75 kg to -9.65 kg. Odds ratios for GI adverse events were similar across compounds/preparations despite high variability in therapeutic efficacy. The study concluded that larger therapeutic efficacy was not systematically associated with higher GI adverse event or drug discontinuation rates. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)
A meta-analysis investigated weight regain after discontinuing GLP-1RA therapy, including semaglutide. Discontinuation of GLP-1RA therapy leads to significant weight regain. Participants taking semaglutide/tirzepatide regained 9.69 kg (95% CI 5.78 to 13.60, P < 0.00001) after discontinuation. Weight regain was proportional to the original weight loss. The study concluded that GLP-1RA treatment should be considered a chronic therapy to prevent weight regain. (Obesity reviews : an official journal of the International Association for the Study of Obesity, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) (STRIDE trial, NCT04560998) involving 792 participants with type 2 diabetes and peripheral artery disease with intermittent claudication investigated semaglutide 1.0 mg once weekly. The estimated median ratio to baseline in maximum walking distance at week 52 was significantly greater in the semaglutide group than the placebo group (1.21 vs 1.08; estimated treatment ratio 1.13 [95% CI 1.06-1.21]; p=0.0004). Safety profile was acceptable, with serious adverse events being infrequent and mainly gastrointestinal, and no treatment-related deaths. This industry-sponsored study concluded that semaglutide increased walking distance in these patients. (Lancet (London, England), 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) (SOUL trial, NCT03914326) involving 9650 participants with type 2 diabetes and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease and/or chronic kidney disease investigated oral semaglutide. Oral semaglutide reduced the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) by 14% compared to placebo (HR 0.86, P=0.006). The incidence of serious adverse events was similar between the oral semaglutide group (47.9%) and the placebo group (50.3%). Confirmatory secondary outcomes, including major kidney disease events, did not differ significantly. This industry-sponsored study concluded that oral semaglutide was associated with a significantly lower risk of MACE without an increase in serious adverse events. (The New England journal of medicine, 2025)
A meta-analysis of 10 trials (n = 71,351) investigated long-acting GLP-1RA, including both injectable and oral formulations, for cardiovascular and kidney outcomes in type 2 diabetes. Long-acting GLP-1RA reduced incidence rate of MACE by 14% (HR 0.86 [95% CI 0.81, 0.90]), HHF by 14% (0.86 [0.79, 0.93]), the composite kidney outcome by 17% (0.83 [0.75, 0.92]), and all-cause mortality by 12% (0.88 [0.82, 0.93]). There was no significant heterogeneity in benefits by GLP-1RA administration route. No increased risks of severe hypoglycemia, retinopathy, or pancreatic events were observed. (Diabetes care, 2025)
A randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 9650 participants investigated oral semaglutide for type 2 diabetes and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease and/or chronic kidney disease. Oral semaglutide reduced the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) by 14% compared to placebo (HR, 0.86; 95% CI, 0.77-0.96) in the overall trial population. The MACE reduction effect was independent of baseline SGLT2i use (P-interaction, 0.66). The adverse event profiles were similar whether or not patients were taking concomitant SGLT2i. This industry-sponsored study concluded that oral semaglutide reduced MACE outcomes independently of concomitant SGLT2i treatment, and the combination appeared safe. (Circulation, 2025)
A post-hoc analysis of RCTs involving 6246 participants compared tirzepatide to placebo, semaglutide 1mg, insulin degludec, or insulin glargine for type 2 diabetes. Median time spent with HbA1c < 7.0% was 80% for tirzepatide versus 70% for semaglutide in 40-week studies. Dose-dependent increases in time spent with ≥5% body weight reduction were observed with tirzepatide (20%-77%) versus 25% with semaglutide 1mg. Tirzepatide-treated participants experienced longer time spent with combined HbA1c ≤ 6.5% and ≥5% body weight reduction versus semaglutide (median: 35%-60% vs. 7%). This industry-sponsored analysis concluded that tirzepatide provided substantially longer continuous time in glycaemic control and more sustained body weight reductions. (Diabetes, obesity & metabolism, 2025)

### Competitive position
Semaglutide faces strong competition, particularly from Eli Lilly's tirzepatide, which has demonstrated superior efficacy in weight reduction and HbA1c levels in comparative analyses. While semaglutide shows robust benefits across a range of indications including obesity, type 2 diabetes, MASH, and cardiovascular outcomes, tirzepatide's dual GIP/GLP-1 agonism appears to offer an edge in some key metabolic parameters. However, semaglutide's established market presence, growing evidence for diverse applications (e.g., post-bariatric surgery weight management, PCOS, KOA pain), and favorable cost-effectiveness profile in many T2D comparisons help maintain its competitive standing. The development of combination therapies like IcoSema (semaglutide with insulin icodec) and CagriSema (semaglutide with cagrilintide) also aims to enhance its efficacy and convenience, providing strong options for treatment intensification and broader patient populations.
