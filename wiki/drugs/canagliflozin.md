### Clinical evidence
A Systematic Review | Network Meta-Analysis published in Diabetes, obesity & metabolism in 2026, involving 80,670 participants, compared various treatments against placebo for composite renal outcome, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). The primary result showed [[dapagliflozin]] 10 mg showing the greatest efficacy (OR 0.55, 95% CI 0.42-0.72; high-certainty evidence) for composite renal outcomes. Secondary results indicated that [[canagliflozin]] 100-300 mg significantly reduced UACR, whereas [[dapagliflozin]] had no effect. None of the novel antidiabetic agents significantly altered eGFR. DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. In patients with T2DM and CKD, SGLT2 inhibitors provide the most consistent renal protection, while GLP-1 receptor agonists offer additional but variable benefits. [[dapagliflozin]] showed the greatest efficacy, and [[canagliflozin]] most strongly reduced albuminuria, highlighting meaningful heterogeneity across agents. DPP-4 inhibitors conferred no renal benefit. Overall, evidence from placebo-controlled trials was robust, whereas certainty was lower for indirect estimates, highlighting the need for drug-specific evaluation in clinical practice.

A meta-analysis published in Clinical nephrology in 2026, involving 98,516 patients, compared SGLT2 inhibitors to placebo for kidney disease progression. SGLT2 inhibitors reduced progressive kidney disease risk in diabetic (OR = 0.64, 95% CI: 0.58 - 0.71) and non-diabetic patients (OR = 0.69, 95% CI: 0.57 - 0.83), with no effect modification by diabetes status (p = 0.49). Among diabetics, risk reductions were notable for [[canagliflozin]] 100mg, [[dapagliflozin]] 10mg, and [[empagliflozin]] 10-mg, particularly in patients with CKD stage 2-3 and baseline eGFR of 30-90 mL/min/1.73m2. Among non-diabetics, [[dapagliflozin]] and [[empagliflozin]] showed consistent benefit. No differences in renal adverse events were observed in either group. DKA risk was elevated in diabetics receiving SGLT2 inhibitors (OR = 2.18, 95% CI: 1.61 - 2.97), particularly with ertugliflozin, sotagliflozin, and [[dapagliflozin]]. SGLT2 inhibitors confer renal protection in both diabetic and non-diabetic populations, supporting their use in CKD management across a broad spectrum of patients. However, careful drug selection is warranted in diabetic patients at risk for DKA.

A Systematic Review | Meta-Analysis published in European journal of preventive cardiology in 2026, encompassing 88273 patients, investigated the impact of SGLT2i on heart failure outcomes in cancer patients. SGLT2i use reduced HF hospitalizations by 51% (RR 0.49, 95% CI 0.36-0.66, I² = 28%, P < 0.01) and new HF diagnoses by 71% (RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.10-0.87, I² = 71%). Multi-variate meta-regression analysis suggested that among breast cancer populations, studies with ≥50% of patients on anthracyclines exhibited a 99% reduction in HF hospitalization risk compared with similar studies that included <50% of patients on anthracyclines (RR 0.0085, 95% CI: 0.0001-0.2645, P = 0.0081). SGLT2i significantly lower the risk of HF hospitalization and new HF diagnoses among cancer patients and survivors, with particularly pronounced benefits in breast cancer patients receiving anthracycline-based chemotherapy. These findings support the need for prospective trials to further investigate the integration of SGLT2i into cancer patient management to enhance cardiovascular outcomes.

A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis published in Frontiers in endocrinology in 2025, involving 14 RCTs, compared various drugs for cardiovascular outcomes (heart failure biomarkers and cardiac function) and metabolic outcomes (glycemic control and body weight) against a competitor drug. SEMA (SMD = -0.22, 95% CI: -1.31 to 0.87) demonstrated the most favorable outcome in reducing BNP levels. LICO (SMD = -0.91, 95% CI: -1.76 to -0.06) ranked highest for body weight reduction. GLIM (SMD = -0.64, 95% CI: -1.12 to -0.17) showed the strongest effect on lowering HbA1c. DAPA (SMD = 0.34, 95% CI: -0.97 to 1.65) was the top-ranked agent for improving LVEF. Safety analysis indicated that LICO and IPRA had the lowest incidence of adverse events. GLIM was associated with an increased risk of hypoglycemia, whereas DAPA was linked to a higher risk of urinary tract infections. The study concluded: SEMA significantly improves both metabolic control and BNP levels, making it suitable for patients requiring comprehensive management of metabolic abnormalities and heart failure. LICO offers a distinct advantage in weight management, particularly benefiting individuals with obesity. DAPA demonstrates notable efficacy in optimizing HbA1c and LVEF, making it a preferred option for patients needing more intensive cardiac support. Despite its moderate efficacy, GLIM remains a viable choice for certain patients due to its favorable safety profile and cost-effectiveness. Collectively, these findings provide essential evidence-based insights to guide individualized therapeutic strategies in type 2 diabetes complicated by heart failure.

A meta-analysis published in The Indian journal of medical research in 2025, involving 205 patients, compared SGLT2i to placebo, metformin, or exenatide for Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS). The primary outcome was total testosterone, with a primary result of mean difference=-0.10 [-0.19, -0.01], P=0.03. Secondary results included free androgen index: mean difference = -0.61 [-1.16, -0.05], P=0.03; homeostasis model assessment-estimated insulin resistance (HOMA-IR): mean difference = -0.38 [-0.75, -0.02], P=0.04; total cholesterol: mean difference=0.13 [0.01, 0.26], P=0.04; and low-density cholesterol: MD=0.18 [0.06, 0.31], P=0.003. Genitourinary AEs were more common in the SGLT2i group (OR=10.88 [1.33, 89.14], P= 0.03). The findings of this meta-analysis suggest that SGLT2i improves the hormonal and glycaemic indices in patients with PCOS. It can prove to be a safe alternative in patients not responding to or intolerant of standard pharmacological treatments.

A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Diabetes research and clinical practice in 2025, involving 17,809 patients, compared SGLT2i to placebo, standard of care, or control. The primary outcomes were all-cause mortality (ACM), cardiovascular death (CVD), all-cause hospitalization (ACH), and hospitalization for heart failure (HHF). Compared to control, SGLT2i significantly reduced ACM (HR 0.87, 95%CI 0.78 to 0.98, low quality of evidence [QoE]), ACH (HR 0.74, 95%CI 0.62 to 0.88, high QoE), and HHF (HR 0.70, 95%CI 0.63 to 0.77, low QoE); but not CVD (HR 0.87, 95%CI 0.76 to 1.00, very low QoE). [[canagliflozin]] ranked highest in decreasing ACM (p-score = 0.86). [[canagliflozin]] ranked highest in decreasing CVD (p-score = 0.82). [[canagliflozin]] ranked highest in decreasing HHF (p-score = 0.88). The study concluded: In patients with HF and T2DM, SGLT2i class effects include ACM, ACH, and HHF reduction. Among SGLT2i, [[canagliflozin]] showed greatest ACM, CVD, and HHF benefit.

A meta-analysis published in Expert opinion on drug safety in 2025, involving 96522 participants (LA: 36 studies) and 102,862 participants (fracture: 66 studies), investigated the risk of limb amputation and bone fractures. An increased risk of LA (OR: 1.2; 95% CI: 1.1, 1.3) was observed. [[canagliflozin]] was associated with increased risk of LA (OR: 1.6, 95% CI: 1.1, 2.4). [[dapagliflozin]] was associated with fracture (OR: 1.1, 95% CI: 1, 1.2). Increased risk of LA (OR: 1.3) among those in the age group of > 40 to < 65, body-mass index of > 30 kg/m2, HbA1c category of > 7%, duration of diabetes of > 10 years, type 2 diabetes. An OR of 1.2 for SGLT2is administration of > 6 months for LA risk. Limb amputations (LA) and fractures were reported in clinical trials and SGLT2is were observed with an increased risk of LA.
