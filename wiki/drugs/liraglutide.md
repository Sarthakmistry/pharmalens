## Liraglutide

**Company:** [[novo-nordisk]] | **Class:** GLP-1 receptor agonist | **Status:** approved

### Approved indications
- GLP-1 Obesity
- Type 2 Diabetes
- HF-HTN

### Pipeline
- GLP-1 Obesity — Phase 3, NCT04775082, ACTIVE_NOT_RECRUITING
- GLP-1 Obesity — Phase 4, NCT05579249, COMPLETED

### Management sentiment
Dapagliflozin restored odour-induced functional integration of the POC-sensorimotor cortex-middle temporal cortex circuit and tended to improve attention, while liraglutide enhanced odour-induced activation in the left hippocampus, indicating positive neuroprotective effects for both drugs. Recent meta-analyses indicate that GLP-1RAs, including liraglutide, are effective adjunctive therapy for weight maintenance post-bariatric surgery and show significant reductions in liver fat content in MASLD/MASH. While GLP-1RAs are associated with an increase in heart rate in non-diabetic individuals with obesity, the overall sentiment remains bullish due to strong efficacy in weight management and metabolic improvements, with GI adverse event rates being similar across compounds despite varying efficacy. The risk of pancreatitis and pancreatic cancer with GLP-1RAs is considered minimal.

### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2026 found that lean mass constituted 26.8% of total weight lost with [[liraglutide]], which was comparable to lifestyle interventions.
A meta-analysis published in Human Psychopharmacology in 2026 found that [[liraglutide]] treatment was associated with a small but significant improvement in psychological well-being (SMD = 0.374) in patients with metabolic disorders, type 2 diabetes, or obesity, but no significant effect on depressive symptoms.
A meta-analysis published in International Journal of Psychiatry in Medicine in 2026 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], significantly reduced weight (MD = 6.17 kg) and HbA1c (MD = 0.31%) in individuals with severe mental illness who have prediabetes or are overweight/obese, with acceptable tolerability.
A systematic review and meta-analysis published in European Journal of Medical Research in 2026 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]] (MD 2.37), were associated with an increase in heart rate in non-diabetic individuals with overweight or obesity.
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2026 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]] or [[semaglutide]], significantly improved functional walking distance (HR=1.10) and reduced the risk of lower extremity amputation (RR=0.53) in people with type 2 diabetes and peripheral artery disease.
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes/Metabolism Research and Reviews in 2026 found that [[liraglutide]] (OR 5.43) was effective in restoring normoglycemia in prediabetes patients with obesity, with 1.8 mg or 3.0 mg daily dosages being effective, and 3.0 mg maintaining post-intervention effectiveness.
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2026 concluded that [[liraglutide]] was not cost-effective for treating obesity without diabetes in high-income countries from a healthcare/payer perspective, with pooled incremental net benefits (INBs) of -$32,032 vs no intervention and -$70,563 vs lifestyle interventions.
A meta-analysis published in Childhood Obesity (Print) in 2026 found that GLP-1 RAs, including [[liraglutide]], improved BMI (-1.7) and showed a modest decrease in ALT levels (-3.0) in youths with obesity.
A systematic review published in International Journal of Obesity (2005) in 2025 found that [[liraglutide]] was associated with increased vomiting risk and increased constipation risk among GLP-1 RAs in non-diabetic individuals with overweight or obesity.
A meta-analysis published in Journal of Diabetes in 2025 found no significant difference in the incidence of suicidal behavior for [[liraglutide]] (RR = 0.92) compared to control groups in patients with type 2 diabetes or obesity.
A meta-analysis published in Medicine in 2025 found no significant statistical difference in suicidal ideations for GLP-1 agonists overall, but a subgroup analysis showed suicide was higher among patients on [[liraglutide]] compared to [[semaglutide]] (OR, 1.48).
A meta-analysis published in Journal of the American College of Cardiology in 2025 found that GLP-1 RAs, including [[liraglutide]], reduced all-cause death (IRR: 0.88), CV death (IRR: 0.87), and MACE (IRR: 0.87) in high-risk populations, but increased gastrointestinal (+63%) and gallbladder (+26%) disorders.
A meta-analysis published in Nature Medicine in 2025 found that all obesity management medications (OMMs), including [[liraglutide]], significantly reduced total body weight compared to placebo.
A meta-analysis published in Obesity (Silver Spring, Md.) in 2025 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], achieved a mean weight loss of -9.0 kg, which was surpassed by [[retatrutide]] and dual agonists (-11.0 kg).
A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Surgical Endoscopy in 2025 found that GLP-1 RA use, including [[liraglutide]], was associated with significantly higher rates of gastric residual content (GRC) and aborted EGDs, but no significant difference in aspiration events. No significant difference in GRC risk was found across different GLP-1 RA agents.
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2025 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], were not cost-effective for obesity treatment in China compared to lifestyle management, with an ICER of $47,994.81.
A meta-analysis published in Journal of Crohn's & Colitis in 2025 found that [[liraglutide]] achieved -9.4 kg weight loss after 3 months in patients with IBD and obesity, and GLP1-RAs were associated with a lower risk of surgery and hospitalizations in obese IBD patients.
A meta-analysis published in Sleep Medicine Reviews in 2025 found that incretin-based therapies, including [[liraglutide]], were associated with significant reductions in body weight, BMI, and AHI (-14.45 events/h) in obesity-related obstructive sleep apnea.
A meta-analysis published in Journal of Obesity in 2025 found that [[tirzepatide]] was superior to GLP-1 RAs, including [[liraglutide]], in weight reduction, though GI side effects were similar.
A meta-analysis published in Endocrinology, Diabetes & Metabolism in 2025 found that GLP-1 RAs, including [[liraglutide]], showed a significantly increased risk of pancreatitis (RR: 1.44), but this risk was not significant when stratified by background medications. No significant association with pancreatic cancer was found overall, and any observed increase with background medications was deemed "likely minimal".
A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Scientific Reports in 2025 found that [[liraglutide]] was third in reducing HbA1c (MD: -1.2%) and FPG (MD: -1.6mmol/L) compared to placebo, and reduced body weight (MD: -1.2 kg). It also reduced the risk of hypoglycemia compared to traditional antidiabetic drugs, making it a potential optimal choice for T2DM treatment to minimize hypoglycemia risk.
A meta-analysis published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2025, analyzing incretin mimetics for type 2 diabetes, concluded that larger therapeutic efficacy (including for [[liraglutide]]) was not systematically associated with higher GI adverse event or drug discontinuation rates, indicating better tolerability for more effective agents.
A meta-analysis published in Obesity Reviews in 2025 showed that discontinuation of [[liraglutide]] therapy led to a weight regain of 2.20 kg, suggesting the need for chronic treatment to prevent weight regain.
A meta-analysis published in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism in 2025 demonstrated that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], significantly reduced liver fat content by 5.21% and improved histological steatosis, hepatocellular ballooning, and lobular inflammation in MASLD and MASH, without worsening fibrosis. No drug-related adverse effects involving the liver were observed.
A systematic review and meta-analysis published in the European Journal of Medical Research in 2026 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]] (MD 2.37), were associated with an increase in heart rate in non-diabetic individuals with overweight or obesity.
A meta-analysis published in the International Journal of Obesity in 2025 indicated that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], are effective adjunctive therapy to maintain weight loss post-bariatric surgery, reducing weight by 8.38 kg and BMI by 3.76 kg/m². However, [[semaglutide]] showed a significantly greater effect on total weight loss compared to liraglutide.
A meta-analysis of 9 RCTs published in Aging Clinical and Experimental Research in 2025, investigating obesity-related knee osteoarthritis (KOA) pain, found that [[liraglutide]] showed a favorable safety profile.
A meta-analysis of 38 phase 3 or 4 placebo-controlled RCTs (16,660 individuals) published in Diabetes, Obesity & Metabolism in 2025, analyzing incretin mimetics for type 2 diabetes, concluded that larger therapeutic efficacy (including for [[liraglutide]]) was not systematically associated with higher GI adverse event or drug discontinuation rates, indicating better tolerability for more effective agents.
A meta-analysis of 2372 participants published in Obesity Reviews in 2025 showed that discontinuation of [[liraglutide]] therapy led to a weight regain of 2.20 kg, suggesting the need for chronic treatment to prevent weight regain.
A meta-analysis of 62 studies (66,232 patients) published in Endocrinology, Diabetes & Metabolism in 2025 indicated a significantly increased risk of pancreatitis with GLP-1 RAs (RR: 1.44), but this risk was not significant when stratified by background medications. No significant association with pancreatic cancer was found overall, and any observed increase with background medications was deemed "likely minimal".
A network meta-analysis published in Clinical Obesity in 2025 found that GLP-1RAs, including [[liraglutide]], significantly lowered systolic blood pressure (SBP). In patients with diabetes, [[liraglutide]] < 2 mg OD showed the greatest SBP reduction (-3.78).
A meta-analysis published in Obesity Surgery in 2025 demonstrated that GLP-1 agonists, including [[liraglutide]], effectively lowered body weight (MD = 8.07 kg) and BMI (MD = 4.42 kg/m2) in patients experiencing weight recurrence or suboptimal clinical response after metabolic bariatric surgery.
A 16-week RCT published in Diabetes, obesity & metabolism in 2025 investigated the effects of [[dapagliflozin]], [[liraglutide]], or acarbose treatment on the directed functional connectivity (FC) of the primary olfactory cortex (POC) circuit and regional activation under odour stimulation. The study included 36 patients per arm (dapagliflozin, liraglutide, acarbose) and 36 healthy controls. After 16 weeks, dapagliflozin restored odour-induced functional integration of the POC-sensorimotor cortex-middle temporal cortex circuit (Gaussian random field correction applied), whereas liraglutide and acarbose did not. Dapagliflozin also tended to improve attention (p = 0.071). Liraglutide enhanced odour-induced activation in the left hippocampus, which was not observed with dapagliflozin and acarbose. The decreased odour-induced directed FC was associated with changes in lipid levels, olfactory threshold, executive function and memory performance (all p < 0.05). These results suggest that dapagliflozin and liraglutide exhibit distinct neuroprotective effects. While liraglutide enhances activation in local olfactory-related regions, dapagliflozin facilitates functional integration within neural circuits. This highlights the importance of targeting both metabolic and neural pathways to manage diabetes-related cognitive decline.
A meta-analysis published in the Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine in 2026, including 200,068 participants, compared GLP-1 receptor agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors regarding the incidence of delirium, depression, dementia or coma during treatment. Among all treatments, only high-dose [[dapagliflozin]] (10 mg/day) significantly reduced the occurrence of delirium and depression, particularly in patients with type 2 diabetes. [[Dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]] were the only agents associated with cognitive improvement. No significant benefits were observed for dementia or coma across all agents. This analysis highlights agent-specific neuroprotective profiles: SGLT2 inhibitors, especially high-dose [[dapagliflozin]], may mitigate the onset of delirium and depression, while GLP-1 receptor agonists, notably [[dulaglutide]] and [[liraglutide]], may enhance cognitive function. These findings warrant consideration in selecting antihyperglycemic therapies for individuals at elevated neuropsychiatric risk.
