### Management sentiment
Durvalumab continues to demonstrate a generally bullish outlook across various oncology indications. Positive trial results, particularly in combination therapies for NSCLC, HCC, and several other cancers, show improved overall survival, progression-free survival, or manageable safety profiles. For instance, the STRIDE regimen (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) in unresectable HCC has sustained OS benefits and continues to set new benchmarks, with participants experiencing immune-mediated adverse events showing improved OS. Similarly, durvalumab plus chemotherapy has significantly improved event-free survival in resectable gastric/gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma and advanced biliary tract cancer, maintaining manageable safety. The addition of durvalumab to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in resectable NSCLC also showed no detrimental effect on surgical feasibility and improved outcomes. While some trials, such as the combination with monalizumab in SCCHN and with olaparib/cediranib in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, did not meet primary endpoints or show significant benefit, and the CALLA trial in locally advanced cervical cancer did not improve PFS, the overall trend from recent clinical data reinforces durvalumab's strong position and potential in diverse cancer treatment landscapes. However, recent meta-analyses on TNBC and advanced squamous NSCLC show mixed results, with benefits observed in specific contexts (e.g., pre-surgery TNBC) but also increased adverse events or no benefit in others, leading to a more neutral overall sentiment.

### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis published in The Cochrane database of systematic reviews in 2026, involving 4341 participants, found that combining PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors (including durvalumab) with chemotherapy before breast cancer surgery significantly improves pathological complete response rate (RR 1.47), event-free survival (HR 0.64), and overall survival (HR 0.56) in early triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) compared to chemotherapy alone. However, this combination also probably increases immune-related serious adverse events (OR 1.75). After breast cancer surgery, there may be little or no difference in EFS and OS, but immune-related SAEs are still increased.
A study published in Signal transduction and targeted therapy in 2026, involving 40 uHCC patients, found that the triple-combination therapy of FOLFOX-HAIC, lenvatinib, and durvalumab exhibited promising therapeutic efficacy, favorable disease control, and a well-tolerated safety profile. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 15.8 months. Objective response rate (ORR) was 75.0% (9 CR/pCR, 21 PR). Disease control rate (DCR) was 95.0%. 1- and 2-year overall survival (OS) rates were 97.5% and 94.0%, respectively. Safety was favorable, with 85.0% experiencing grade 1-2 adverse events and no grade 4 adverse events observed.
A study published in Lung cancer (Amsterdam, Netherlands) in 2026 found that neoadjuvant durvalumab plus chemotherapy was feasible with low toxicity, high rates of mediastinal nodal clearance (N2NC 73.3% vs historical 30%), and complete resection (R0 93.3%) in surgically challenging, documented N2+ NSCLC. Pathologic complete response (pCR) was 30%. Event-free survival (EFS) at 18 months was 60.6% overall and 73.4% for the resected cohort. 16% of patients experienced grade 3 and higher treatment-related adverse events, primarily lymphopenia.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2026, involving 41 patients, found that savolitinib plus durvalumab shows overall survival (OS) in MET-driven papillary renal cancer (PRC), supporting the ongoing SAMETA RIII trial. Overall response rate (ORR) was 34% in the intention-to-treat (ITT) population and 53% in MET-driven patients. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 6.5 months in ITT and 13.9 months in MET-driven patients. Median OS was 18.3 months in ITT and 27.4 months in MET-driven patients. Baseline ctDNA positivity correlated with shorter OS (median 7.3 vs 33.3 months). ctDNA clearance and mean variant allele frequency reduction correlated with improved OS.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2026, involving 41 evaluable patients, found that doxorubicin plus durvalumab combination therapy exhibited promising efficacy in advanced Soft Tissue Sarcoma (STS), with an acceptable toxicity profile. The objective response rate (ORR) was 31.7% (1 complete response, 12 partial responses). Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 7.6 months. Median overall survival was 23.8 months. The most common treatment-related grade 3 to 4 adverse events were neutropenia (53.4%), thrombocytopenia (13.9%), and anemia (11.6%). Absence of RTK-RAS pathway genetic alterations and high PD-1 expression were identified as independent predictors of longer PFS.
A study published in Journal for immunotherapy of cancer in 2026 found that olaparib plus durvalumab demonstrated modest activity in HRR-unselected metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), with clinical benefit enriched in BRCA2-variant disease. Median radiographic progression-free survival (rPFS) was 5.0 months and median overall survival (OS) was 19.1 months. Patients with BRCA2 variants experienced longer rPFS (13.2 months) than patients without BRCA2 variants (4.8 months, p=0.0026). Baseline ctDNA fraction was higher in patients with radiographic progression than in those with partial response (p=0.022). Lower IL1R2 expression was associated with longer OS.
A study published in Cancer in 2026, involving 51 patients with limited-stage small cell lung cancer, found that the addition of durvalumab to chemoradiotherapy was well tolerated and showed encouraging efficacy. The 1-, 2-, and 3-year progression-free survival (PFS) rates were 56.9%, 35.9%, and 28.2%, respectively, with a median PFS of 17.0 months. The 1-, 2-, and 3-year overall survival (OS) rates were 88.1%, 68.6%, and 49.6%, respectively, with a median OS of 32.0 months. 17 (33.4%) patients experienced grade 3 or 4 adverse events, with 7 being immune-related.
A study published in Breast cancer research and treatment in 2026 found that neoadjuvant durvalumab plus AI was safe but demonstrated limited pathologic efficacy in unselected HR+ HER2-negative breast cancer. Among the first 14 patients, one achieved mPEPI 0, which did not meet criteria to proceed to stage two. Overall, three patients (17.6%) achieved mPEPI 0. A clinical complete response was seen in 58.8% of patients, although no pathologic complete responses occurred.
A study published in Frontiers in immunology in 2026, involving 13572 patients, compared various ICI-based combinations against sorafenib for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS) in advanced HCC. In non-viral HCC, the STRIDE regimen (single priming dose tremelimumab plus durvalumab) was the only regimen to significantly improve OS (HR = 0.75, 0.59-0.96).
A study published in Gynecologic oncology in 2026 found that the DUO-E pMMR subpopulation was highly heterogeneous with frequent overlap of biomarkers and histology. Consistent with the primary analysis, durvalumab plus olaparib improved the PFS benefit observed with durvalumab versus control across a range of biomarker and histological subgroups.
A study published in The Cochrane database of systematic reviews in 2026 involving seven RCTs with 4341 participants, including trials with durvalumab, found that combining PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy before breast cancer surgery significantly improves pathological complete response rate (RR 1.47), event-free survival (HR 0.64), and overall survival (HR 0.56) in early triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) compared to chemotherapy alone. However, this combination also probably increases immune-related serious adverse events (OR 1.75). After breast cancer surgery, there may be little or no difference in EFS and OS, but immune-related SAEs are still increased.
A study published in British journal of clinical pharmacology in 2026 found that the PopPK and ER analyses support the novel perioperative durvalumab regimen for patients with resectable NSCLC from the AEGEAN study, and no dose adjustment is necessary. At the 1500 mg dose, a flat relationship was observed between durvalumab PK exposure and efficacy endpoints (EFS and pCR), and no clinically relevant relationships were observed between durvalumab PK exposure and safety endpoints.
A study published in Urologic oncology in 2026, a network meta-analysis of 12 randomized controlled trials with 5,026 patients with MIBC, found that the most effective strategy for MIBC was NAC followed by surgery and adjuvant nivolumab (SUCRA: 99%, HR 0.43, 95% CrI 0.24-0.74). Perioperative durvalumab-based regimens (SUCRA: 82%) and dose-dense MVAC (SUCRA: 78%) also demonstrated significant benefit.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2026 found that the DUO-O trial met its primary PFS endpoints for first-line durvalumab plus carboplatin/paclitaxel and bevacizumab followed by durvalumab, bevacizumab plus olaparib maintenance versus carboplatin/paclitaxel and bevacizumab followed by bevacizumab in the non-tBRCAm HRD-positive and non-tBRCAm ITT populations. PFS HR for durvalumab + olaparib arm versus control was 0.49 (95% CI 0.34-0.69, P < 0.0001; median PFS 37.3 versus 23.0 months) in the non-tBRCAm HRD-positive population. Safety was generally consistent with the profiles of the individual agents.
A study published in Cell reports. Medicine in 2025, involving 57 pre- and post-treatment tumor biopsies from head and neck cancer patients treated with durvalumab alone or with durvalumab plus tremelimumab, found that malignant cells and neutrophil senescence promote ICI response, while anti-CTLA-4 induces 4-1BB+ regulatory T cells (Tregs) that restrict ICI efficacy. Two subsets of tumor-reactive progenitor Tex (Tpex) were identified: ICI-responsive Tpex1 and ICI-resistant Tpex2. The balance of Tpex1 and Tpex2 associates with ICI response across multiple cancers.
A study published in The Lancet. Oncology in 2025 (NCT04153734) found that radiotherapy-free pembrolizumab and chemotherapy might provide a feasible and promising alternative treatment strategy for patients with unresectable, locally advanced NSCLC with a PD-L1 TPS of 50% or higher. The 2-year progression-free survival was 67% (90% CI 46-83). No treatment-related deaths were reported.
A study published in Journal of clinical oncology : official journal of the American Society of Clinical Oncology in 2025 found that durvalumab administered from the start of cCRT failed to demonstrate additional benefit compared with cCRT plus placebo in unresectable stage III NSCLC. No statistically significant difference in PFS (HR, 0.85 [95% CI, 0.65 to 1.12]; P = .247) or OS (HR, 1.03 [95% CI, 0.78 to 1.39]; P = .823) was observed. AEs leading to discontinuation occurred in 25.6% of durvalumab patients versus 12.0% of placebo patients.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 found that metronomic vinorelbine in combination with dual durvalumab plus tremelimumab immunotherapy had only moderate activity in pretreated advanced HNSCC. The clinical benefit rate (CBR) was 23.5% (95% credible interval 7.3-45.6), and the objective response rate was 14.3%. Median progression-free survival was 1.8 months and median overall survival was 8 months.
A study published in Nature communications in 2025 (ACTRN12618000686202) on the SOLACE2 trial found that priming with olaparib, or cyclophosphamide-olaparib, followed by durvalumab-olaparib, are both associated with longer PFS compared to olaparib monotherapy in platinum-sensitive recurrent ovarian cancer. However, the trial did not reach the pre-specified primary endpoint of 36-week trial threshold (PFS36). A prognostic blood signature (CUP-CC) was identified with potential for personalizing therapy.
A study published in International journal of radiation oncology, biology, physics in 2025, involving 35 patients with oligometastatic NSCLC, found that the combination of durvalumab, chemotherapy, and SABR was effective and tolerable. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 10.4 months. The 2-year PFS rate was 38.5% and the 2-year overall survival (OS) rate was 67.7%. Subgroup analysis showed median PFS of 24.3 months with SABR versus 3.1 months without SABR (HR, 0.2; P < .001). Grade 3 or higher treatment-related adverse events occurred in 57.1% of patients.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 (NCT04053322) on the DOLAF trial found that durvalumab, olaparib, and fulvestrant in ER+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer patients with selected genomic alterations was active and had an acceptable toxicity profile. The 24-week progression-free survival rate was 66.7% and median overall survival was 30 months.
A study published in Cancer science in 2025 (NCT03703297) found that consolidation durvalumab demonstrated a favorable risk/benefit profile in Japanese patients with LS-SCLC post cCRT. A study published in Urologic oncology in 2025 found that ICI-chemotherapy combination therapy, including durvalumab, was associated with a significantly higher pathological complete response (pCR) rate (40.6%) compared to chemotherapy alone (17.9%) in neoadjuvant muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC). A study published in Breast (Edinburgh, Scotland) in 2025 (NCT03518606) on the MOVIE study found that tremelimumab plus durvalumab combined with metronomic oral vinorelbine showed limited antitumor activity in pretreated advanced breast cancer. A study published in Nature communications in 2025, involving 38 patients with non-resectable stage IIIA-IIIC NSCLC, evaluated a regimen including durvalumab. The primary outcome was 12-month progression-free survival (PFS). PFS was 68.4% (95% CI, 51.1-80.7) at 12 months (statistically significant compared to null hypothesis of 55%). Secondary results showed PFS was 50.0% at 24 months, OS was 86.8% at 12 months and 60.5% at 24 months. After induction, 18 (47.4%) had partial response, 14 (36.8%) stable disease, and 5 (13.2%) progressive disease. Overall, 18 patients had disease progression: 8 local (44.4%) and 10 distant (55.6%). Grade ≥3 TRAEs occurred in 11 (28.9%) during induction, 10 (26.3%) during CT-RT, and 2 (5.3%) during maintenance. ChIO induction before CT-RT and IO maintenance showed activity and safety, warranting confirmation in larger studies.
An observational study involving 88 patients evaluated the safety and recommended phase 2 dose of tarlatamab in combination with a PD-L1 inhibitor. The most common grade 3-4 adverse events were hyponatraemia (nine [10%] of 88 patients), anaemia (seven [8%] of 88 patients), and neutropenia (six [7%] of 88 patients). Serious adverse events occurred in 50 (57%) of 88 patients. The most common serious adverse events were cytokine release syndrome (21 [24%] of 88 patients), pyrexia (six [7%] of 88 patients), immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (four [5%] of 88 patients), and pneumonia (four [5%] of 88 patients). There were no deaths due to treatment-related adverse events. Median overall survival was 25.3 months (95% CI 20.3-not estimable). The study concluded: Tarlatamab plus a PD-L1 inhibitor as maintenance after first-line chemo-immunotherapy showed a manageable safety profile with promising anticancer activity, supporting the ongoing phase 3 trial (NCT06211036).
A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis published in Frontiers in immunology in 2025, including 25 randomized controlled trials, compared various immune checkpoint inhibitors against chemotherapy alone for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). Cemiplimab [HR = 0.48, 95% CI: (0.34-0.67)] showed the best OS benefit, while Sugemalimab+chemo provided the best PFS benefit [HR = 0.34, 95% CI: (0.24-0.48)]. For PD-L1≥50% tumors, penpulimab showed excellent OS and PFS. For PD-L1 1-49% tumors, pembrolizumab+chemo and camrelizumab+chemo achieved the best OS and PFS, respectively. For PD-L1≥1% tumors, tislelizumab+chemo and camrelizumab+chemo showed the best OS and PFS results. For tumors with PD-L1 <1%, nivolumab and serplulimab+chemo provided significant survival benefit. In Asian patients, pembrolizumab or pembrolizumab + chemotherapy had favorable OS and PFS benefits. In non-Asian patients, cemiplimab showed favorable OS and PFS benefit. For former/current smokers, pembrolizumab+chemotherapy and camrelizumab+chemotherapy had significant OS and PFS benefit. Camrelizumab+chemo [OR = 3.5, 95% CI: (2.3-5.3)] had the best overall response rate (ORR) benefit. Ipilimumab+chemo had the highest incidence of adverse events (AEs) [OR = 2.0, 95% CI:(1.5-2.7)].
A study published in JAMA network open in 2025 (NCT03822351) on the COAST trial, an open-label, phase 2, multidrug platform randomized clinical trial involving 189 patients with unresectable, stage III NSCLC without progression following cCRT, found that combining consolidation durvalumab with oleclumab or monalizumab provided additional clinical benefit over durvalumab alone. The confirmed objective response rate (ORR) was numerically higher with durvalumab plus oleclumab (35.0%) or monalizumab (40.3%) compared to durvalumab alone (23.9%). Both combinations prolonged progression-free survival (PFS) vs durvalumab alone (oleclumab HR, 0.59; monalizumab HR, 0.63). Safety was comparable across all treatment arms without new or notable safety signals. These findings support further investigation of these novel combinations in the phase 3 PACIFIC-9 trial.
A study published in CPT: pharmacometrics & systems pharmacology in 2025 (NCT04015739) on the BOLD phase II trial investigated a triplet regimen (bevacizumab, olaparib, durvalumab) in 74 patients with recurrent platinum-sensitive/resistant advanced ovarian cancer. The study found that the prognostic value of a joint model integrating CA-125 kinetics and tumor size was not clinically different from that of CA-125 kinetics alone for PFS and OS. The improvement in prognostic value was not sufficient to justify the higher complexity of the joint model, suggesting that assessing early longitudinal CA-125 kinetics alone remains the best pragmatic strategy.
A study published in The oncologist in 2025 (NCT03875235) found that durvalumab plus gemcitabine and cisplatin demonstrated an overall survival (OS) benefit versus placebo in advanced biliary tract cancer (aBTC), irrespective of immune-mediated adverse events (imAEs). imAEs were reported in 13.9% of participants in the durvalumab arm versus 4.7% in the placebo arm, with a median time to onset of 127.0 days for durvalumab. The OS HR for durvalumab versus placebo in participants with imAEs was 0.59 (95% CI, 0.30-1.23), and in participants without imAEs was 0.83 (95% CI, 0.70-1.00). imAEs were mostly low grade and manageable.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 found that durvalumab combined with platinum-etoposide chemotherapy demonstrated promising intracranial activity in SCLC patients with untreated brain metastases. The intracranial response rate (modified RECIST) was 66.7% (90% CI 54.0% to 77.3%), meeting the study's primary endpoint. The median intracranial progression-free survival was 4.2 months, and the median time to local brain therapy was 10.4 months. However, only a small proportion of patients achieved a sustained response, highlighting the need for regular monitoring.
A study published in Lung cancer (Amsterdam, Netherlands) in 2025 on the REBORN study found that irinotecan plus cisplatin (IP) plus durvalumab in untreated extensive-stage small cell lung cancer (ES-SCLC) did not demonstrate the expected efficacy, with its efficacy and safety generally comparable to those of EP plus durvalumab. The 12-month PFS rate by ICR was 18.8% (90% CI, 9.3-30.8%), with a median PFS of 5.7 months. Median OS was 16.9 months. Grade ≥3 adverse events (AEs) occurred in 73.8% of patients, including two grade 5 AEs (pneumonitis and hepatitis).
A study published in Journal of gynecologic oncology in 2025 (NCT04269200) on a Japan subset analysis of the DUO-E trial found that efficacy and safety of carboplatin/paclitaxel + durvalumab followed by durvalumab with or without olaparib were generally consistent with outcomes in the DUO-E ITT population for advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer. Progression-free survival (PFS) favored durvalumab (HR=0.61) and durvalumab + olaparib (HR=0.44) compared to control, with median PFS of 9.9 months and 15.1 months respectively, versus 9.5 months for control. The safety profile was consistent with established profiles of individual agents.
A study published in Blood advances in 2025 (NCT03610061) on a Phase 1 study of RT-durvalumab in 34 adults with relapsed/refractory DLBCL and FL found that RT-durvalumab with 30 Gy/10 fractions of RT to 3 disease sites is safe, and offers promising responses in FL. The overall response rate was 60% in FL (40% complete response) and 14% in DLBCL (7% complete response). Common grade 3-4 toxicities included anemia (9%), neutropenia (11%), and liver dysfunction (5%). Intratumoral and peripheral blood CD8 T-cell dysregulation correlated with treatment response.
A study published in The oncologist in 2025 (NCT03323398) on a first-in-human, phase I/II study of mRNA-2416 alone or with durvalumab in patients with advanced solid tumors or lymphoma found that the treatment was well tolerated, with primarily grade 1/2 treatment-related emergent adverse events. On-treatment tumor biopsies demonstrated increased OX40L protein expression, elevated PD-L1, and proinflammatory responses, with tumor shrinkage occurring in injected and surrounding non-injected tumors. However, predefined primary efficacy endpoints were not met in an exploratory cohort of ovarian cancer, warranting additional research.
A study published in Annals of oncology : official journal of the European Society for Medical Oncology in 2025 (NCT03830866) on the CALLA trial investigated durvalumab + chemoradiotherapy (CRT) versus CRT alone in locally advanced cervical cancer (LACC). The addition of durvalumab did not significantly improve progression-free survival (PFS) in the intent-to-treat population. However, ultrasensitive circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) detection was identified as a potential predictive and prognostic biomarker for disease progression and survival in LACC, independent of disease stage. ctDNA detection at C3D1 (post-CRT) occurred a median of 164 days before clinical progression.
A study published in The New England journal of medicine in 2025 (NCT04592913) on the MATTERHORN trial found that perioperative durvalumab plus FLOT led to significantly better event-free survival outcomes than FLOT alone among participants with resectable gastric or gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma. Two-year event-free survival was 67.4% in the durvalumab group vs 58.5% in the placebo group (HR 0.71; P<0.001). Two-year overall survival was 75.7% in the durvalumab group vs 70.4% in the placebo group (HR 0.67 from month 12 onward; P=0.03). The pathological complete response rate was 19.2% in the durvalumab group vs 7.2% in the placebo group. Adverse events (grade 3 or 4) were similar between groups (71.6% vs 71.2%).
A study published in Nature medicine in 2025 (NCT05061550) on the NeoCOAST-2 platform study, which randomized 202 patients with untreated, resectable stage IIA-IIIB NSCLC, found that pathological complete response (pCR) rates were highest in the datopotamab-deruxtecan-containing arm (35.2%) compared to durvalumab + platinum-doublet chemotherapy + oleclumab (20.3%) or monalizumab (25.7%). Major pathological response (mPR) rates followed a similar trend. Grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events were lowest in the datopotamab-deruxtecan arm (20.4%). These results warrant further investigation of ADCs and checkpoint inhibition in the neoadjuvant setting.
A study published in Urology in 2025 (NCT02762006) found that surgery for locally advanced renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is safe for patients after neoadjuvant durvalumab +/- tremelimumab. Twenty-five patients underwent neo-adjuvant therapy prior to radical/partial nephrectomy. Median estimated blood loss was 400cc, with 3 patients requiring postoperative transfusion. No intraoperative complications occurred, and intraoperative adhesions were noted in 16% of cases.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 (NCT03693300) on the PACIFIC-6 trial, involving 117 patients with unresectable stage III non-small-cell lung cancer treated with durvalumab after sCRT, reported that 27.4% of patients experienced grade 3/4 adverse events (AEs) and 6.0% experienced grade 3/4 possibly related AEs. Median overall survival (OS) was 39.0 months, with a 3-year OS rate of 56.5%. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 13.1 months, with a 2-year PFS rate of 35.3%. The study concluded that durvalumab after sCRT was well tolerated and could be an alternative treatment strategy when cCRT is not feasible.
A study published in British journal of cancer in 2025 (NCT03751761) on a multicenter phase Ib/II trial evaluating durvalumab, tremelimumab, and paclitaxel as second-line treatment for biomarker-selected metastatic gastric cancer found the combination to be tolerable and efficacious. The objective response rate (ORR) was 52.1%, meeting the primary endpoint. The median progression-free survival (PFS) was 5.3 months and overall survival (OS) was 13.1 months. The most common any-grade adverse event was anemia (41.7%), and the most common grade 3-4 adverse event was neutropenia (10.4%).
A study published in Journal of hepatology in 2025 (NCT03875235) on the long-term follow-up of durvalumab plus gemcitabine and cisplatin (GemCis) in advanced biliary tract cancer (aBTC) found that survival benefit and manageable safety continued approximately 3 years after the last participant was randomized. Median OS for durvalumab+GemCis was 12.9 months vs. 11.3 months for placebo+GemCis (HR, 0.74). The 36-month OS rate was 14.6% for durvalumab+GemCis vs. 6.9% for placebo+GemCis. Overall, 12.8% of participants were extended long-term survivors (eLTS), with more in the durvalumab+GemCis arm (17.0%).
A study published in Hepatology (Baltimore, Md.) in 2026 found that participants who experienced immune-mediated adverse events (imAEs) with STRIDE (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) had a numerical improvement in OS versus those who did not, and results continue to support the benefits of STRIDE in unresectable HCC. The OS Hazard Ratio (95% CI) for STRIDE in participants with imAEs versus those without was 0.73 (0.56-0.95). The 36-month OS rate for STRIDE was 36.2% (with imAEs) and 27.7% (without imAEs). Most imAEs were low grade, manageable, and occurred within the first 3 months after treatment initiation.
A study published in Future oncology (London, England) in 2025 (NCT03043872) reported an exploratory analysis of patient-reported adverse events (AEs) using PRO-CTCAE data from a subset of 164 patients in the CASPIAN study, which evaluated durvalumab plus platinum-etoposide (EP) in extensive-stage small-cell lung cancer (ES-SCLC). The study found that AE rates and patterns reported by patients were generally comparable between the durvalumab + EP and EP arms over the first 24 weeks, with most reported AEs occurring rarely/occasionally and being mild/moderate in severity. This suggests that durvalumab improved survival without adversely affecting patients’ perspectives on side effects and quality of life.
A study published in Lung cancer (Amsterdam, Netherlands) in 2025 on the CHESS phase II trial, which enrolled 49 patients with synchronous oligometastatic non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) treated with durvalumab, carboplatin, paclitaxel, and tremelimumab, did not meet its primary endpoint of one-year progression-free survival (33% achieved vs. 50% target). However, the one-year overall survival rate was 74.9%, and treatment-related grade ≥3 adverse events occurred in 34% of patients with no grade 5 events. The favorable safety profile and promising overall survival provided the basis for further intensification of induction systemic therapy.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 (EORTC-HNCG-1559) on the I2 substudy of the UPSTREAM trial evaluated monalizumab + durvalumab (D+M) versus physician's choice in heavily pretreated R/M SCCHN patients previously exposed to anti-PD(L)1. The study failed to demonstrate activity of D+M, with only one partial response in the D+M arm and no benefit in PFS (2.0 months vs 3.1 months in control) and OS (4.3 months vs 8.0 months in control).
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 found that the combination of neoadjuvant durvalumab and olaparib shows therapeutic activity in bladder cancer. In a phase II clinical trial involving 29 patients with resectable urothelial bladder cancer, a pathologic complete response was observed in 13 cases (44.8%), with no major safety concerns. Resistance mechanisms appeared to be driven by transcriptional adaptations rather than new mutations.
A study published in European urology oncology in 2025 found that ICI-chemotherapy combination therapy, including durvalumab, was associated with a significantly higher pathological complete response (pCR) rate (40.6%) compared to chemotherapy alone (17.9%) in neoadjuvant muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC). However, two phase 3 RCTs showed no significant difference in overall survival (OS) between durvalumab + gemcitabine + cisplatin (GC) and dose-dense methotrexate + vinblastine + Adriamycin + cisplatin (ddMVAC). ddMVAC significantly increased the risk of grade ≥3 anemia and asthenia compared to durvalumab + GC.
A study published in Gynecologic oncology in 2025 (NCT03277482) on a phase I clinical trial investigating radiation therapy (RT), durvalumab, and tremelimumab for recurrent gynecologic cancer found limited dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs), indicating acceptable safety. Zero DLTs were observed in cohort A and one DLT in cohort B. A study published in Journal of hepatology in 2025 (NCT03298451) on the 5-year overall survival (OS) data for STRIDE (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) versus sorafenib in unresectable HCC found that STRIDE sustained an OS benefit vs. sorafenib. A study published in Journal of thoracic oncology : official publication of the International Association for the Study of Lung Cancer in 2025 (WJOG 12119L) on durvalumab plus carboplatin/paclitaxel in resectable Stage III N2 NSCLC found that the primary endpoint, major pathologic response (MPR), was 63%, which met the target. The 12-month PFS rate was 65.9%, and the 30-month overall survival rate was 87.7%. After NAC, the objective response rate was 86.7%, with 11.1% achieving pathologic complete response. A study published in Gut in 2025 (NCT02821754, 2019-002767-98) on a Phase II clinical trial of tremelimumab plus durvalumab in 28 HCC patients found a median overall survival of 19.2 months. Tumour tissue analysis revealed enhanced interferon responses and antigen presentation in responders. Non-responder tumours had higher numbers of Tregs located in immune cell-enriched neighborhoods and expressed higher levels of ICOS and PD-1. The study provides a blueprint for in-depth analysis of immune correlates in immunotherapy and demonstrates the importance of Treg distribution in HCC.
A study published in Blood advances in 2025 (NCT03011814) on a Phase 1/2 clinical trial of durvalumab and lenalidomide in relapsed/refractory cutaneous T-cell lymphoma found the combination showed clinical activity with 7 partial responses and 4 stable disease, achieving best overall and skin response rates of 58.3% and 75% respectively, with a median duration of response of 25.5 months. A study published in Journal of clinical oncology : official journal of the American Society of Clinical Oncology in 2025 (NCT04617756) on the iNDUCT-GETUG V08 phase II trial evaluating neoadjuvant durvalumab plus gemcitabine/cisplatin (cohort 1) or carboplatin (cohort 2) in high-risk operable Upper Tract Urothelial Carcinoma (UTUC) found that the study did not meet its primary endpoint of pathologic complete response (pT0) rate (13% in cohort 1, 5% in cohort 2). ### Competitive position
Durvalumab continues to hold a strong position in various oncology indications, particularly in NSCLC and HCC, often demonstrating superior outcomes in combination therapies. In advanced biliary tract cancer, durvalumab + gemcitabine/cisplatin is confirmed as the best available treatment. For unresectable HCC, the STRIDE regimen (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) shows sustained OS benefits, especially in non-viral HCC, and distinct mechanisms of action compared to durvalumab plus bevacizumab. In resectable gastric/gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma, perioperative durvalumab plus FLOT significantly improves event-free survival. However, in some areas, competition is intense. For instance, in locally advanced cervical cancer, pembrolizumab appears to rank higher in OS and PFS compared to durvalumab-containing regimens. In platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, durvalumab combinations with olaparib and cediranib have failed to meet primary endpoints, and in recurrent ovarian clear cell carcinoma, durvalumab did not improve efficacy over chemotherapy. In advanced breast cancer, combinations with durvalumab have shown limited antitumor activity. While durvalumab shows promise in specific contexts like muscle-invasive bladder cancer (with recent FDA approval for neoadjuvant use) and limited-stage SCLC, its efficacy can be highly dependent on the specific cancer type, stage, and combination partners, indicating a nuanced competitive landscape.
