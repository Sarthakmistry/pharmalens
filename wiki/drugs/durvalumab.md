### Management sentiment
Durvalumab continues to demonstrate a generally bullish outlook across various oncology indications. Positive trial results, particularly in combination therapies for NSCLC, HCC, and several other cancers, show improved overall survival, progression-free survival, or manageable safety profiles. For instance, the STRIDE regimen (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) in unresectable HCC has sustained OS benefits and continues to set new benchmarks, with participants experiencing immune-mediated adverse events showing improved OS. Similarly, durvalumab plus chemotherapy has significantly improved event-free survival in resectable gastric/gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma and advanced biliary tract cancer, maintaining manageable safety. The addition of durvalumab to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in resectable NSCLC also showed no detrimental effect on surgical feasibility and improved outcomes. While some trials, such as the combination with monalizumab in SCCHN and with olaparib/cediranib in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, did not meet primary endpoints or show significant benefit, and the CALLA trial in locally advanced cervical cancer did not improve PFS, the overall trend from recent clinical data reinforces durvalumab's strong position and potential in diverse cancer treatment landscapes.

### Clinical evidence
A study published in JAMA network open in 2025 (NCT03822351) on the COAST trial, an open-label, phase 2, multidrug platform randomized clinical trial involving 189 patients with unresectable, stage III NSCLC without progression following cCRT, found that combining consolidation durvalumab with oleclumab or monalizumab provided additional clinical benefit over durvalumab alone. The confirmed objective response rate (ORR) was numerically higher with durvalumab plus oleclumab (35.0%) or monalizumab (40.3%) compared to durvalumab alone (23.9%). Both combinations prolonged progression-free survival (PFS) vs durvalumab alone (oleclumab HR, 0.59; monalizumab HR, 0.63). Safety was comparable across all treatment arms without new or notable safety signals. These findings support further investigation of these novel combinations in the phase 3 PACIFIC-9 trial.
A study published in CPT: pharmacometrics & systems pharmacology in 2025 (NCT04015739) on the BOLD phase II trial investigated a triplet regimen (bevacizumab, olaparib, durvalumab) in 74 patients with recurrent platinum-sensitive/resistant advanced ovarian cancer. The study found that the prognostic value of a joint model integrating CA-125 kinetics and tumor size was not clinically different from that of CA-125 kinetics alone for PFS and OS. The improvement in prognostic value was not sufficient to justify the higher complexity of the joint model, suggesting that assessing early longitudinal CA-125 kinetics alone remains the best pragmatic strategy.
A study published in The oncologist in 2025 (NCT03875235) found that durvalumab plus gemcitabine and cisplatin demonstrated an overall survival (OS) benefit versus placebo in advanced biliary tract cancer (aBTC), irrespective of immune-mediated adverse events (imAEs). imAEs were reported in 13.9% of participants in the durvalumab arm versus 4.7% in the placebo arm, with a median time to onset of 127.0 days for durvalumab. The OS HR for durvalumab versus placebo in participants with imAEs was 0.59 (95% CI, 0.30-1.23), and in participants without imAEs was 0.83 (95% CI, 0.70-1.00). imAEs were mostly low grade and manageable.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 found that durvalumab combined with platinum-etoposide chemotherapy demonstrated promising intracranial activity in SCLC patients with untreated brain metastases. The intracranial response rate (modified RECIST) was 66.7% (90% CI 54.0% to 77.3%), meeting the study's primary endpoint. The median intracranial progression-free survival was 4.2 months, and the median time to local brain therapy was 10.4 months. However, only a small proportion of patients achieved a sustained response, highlighting the need for regular monitoring.
A study published in Lung cancer (Amsterdam, Netherlands) in 2025 on the REBORN study found that irinotecan plus cisplatin (IP) plus durvalumab in untreated extensive-stage small cell lung cancer (ES-SCLC) did not demonstrate the expected efficacy, with its efficacy and safety generally comparable to those of EP plus durvalumab. The 12-month PFS rate by ICR was 18.8% (90% CI, 9.3-30.8%), with a median PFS of 5.7 months. Median OS was 16.9 months. Grade ≥3 adverse events (AEs) occurred in 73.8% of patients, including two grade 5 AEs (pneumonitis and hepatitis).
A study published in Journal of gynecologic oncology in 2025 (NCT04269200) on a Japan subset analysis of the DUO-E trial found that efficacy and safety of carboplatin/paclitaxel + durvalumab followed by durvalumab with or without olaparib were generally consistent with outcomes in the DUO-E ITT population for advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer. Progression-free survival (PFS) favored durvalumab (HR=0.61) and durvalumab + olaparib (HR=0.44) compared to control, with median PFS of 9.9 months and 15.1 months respectively, versus 9.5 months for control. The safety profile was consistent with established profiles of individual agents.
A study published in Blood advances in 2025 (NCT03610061) on a Phase 1 study of RT-durvalumab in 34 adults with relapsed/refractory DLBCL and FL found that RT-durvalumab with 30 Gy/10 fractions of RT to 3 disease sites is safe, and offers promising responses in FL. The overall response rate was 60% in FL (40% complete response) and 14% in DLBCL (7% complete response). Common grade 3-4 toxicities included anemia (9%), neutropenia (11%), and liver dysfunction (5%). Intratumoral and peripheral blood CD8 T-cell dysregulation correlated with treatment response.
A study published in The oncologist in 2025 (NCT03323398) on a first-in-human, phase I/II study of mRNA-2416 alone or with durvalumab in patients with advanced solid tumors or lymphoma found that the treatment was well tolerated, with primarily grade 1/2 treatment-related emergent adverse events. On-treatment tumor biopsies demonstrated increased OX40L protein expression, elevated PD-L1, and proinflammatory responses, with tumor shrinkage occurring in injected and surrounding non-injected tumors. However, predefined primary efficacy endpoints were not met in an exploratory cohort of ovarian cancer, warranting additional research.
A study published in Annals of oncology : official journal of the European Society for Medical Oncology in 2025 (NCT03830866) on the CALLA trial investigated durvalumab + chemoradiotherapy (CRT) versus CRT alone in locally advanced cervical cancer (LACC). The addition of durvalumab did not significantly improve progression-free survival (PFS) in the intent-to-treat population. However, ultrasensitive circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) detection was identified as a potential predictive and prognostic biomarker for disease progression and survival in LACC, independent of disease stage. ctDNA detection at C3D1 (post-CRT) occurred a median of 164 days before clinical progression.
A study published in The New England journal of medicine in 2025 (NCT04592913) on the MATTERHORN trial found that perioperative durvalumab plus FLOT led to significantly better event-free survival outcomes than FLOT alone among participants with resectable gastric or gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma. Two-year event-free survival was 67.4% in the durvalumab group vs 58.5% in the placebo group (HR 0.71; P<0.001). Two-year overall survival was 75.7% in the durvalumab group vs 70.4% in the placebo group (HR 0.67 from month 12 onward; P=0.03). The pathological complete response rate was 19.2% in the durvalumab group vs 7.2% in the placebo group. Adverse events (grade 3 or 4) were similar between groups (71.6% vs 71.2%).
A study published in Nature medicine in 2025 (NCT05061550) on the NeoCOAST-2 platform study, which randomized 202 patients with untreated, resectable stage IIA-IIIB NSCLC, found that pathological complete response (pCR) rates were highest in the datopotamab-deruxtecan-containing arm (35.2%) compared to durvalumab + platinum-doublet chemotherapy + oleclumab (20.3%) or monalizumab (25.7%). Major pathological response (mPR) rates followed a similar trend. Grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events were lowest in the datopotamab-deruxtecan arm (20.4%). These results warrant further investigation of ADCs and checkpoint inhibition in the neoadjuvant setting.
A study published in Urology in 2025 (NCT02762006) found that surgery for locally advanced renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is safe for patients after neoadjuvant durvalumab +/- tremelimumab. Twenty-five patients underwent neo-adjuvant therapy prior to radical/partial nephrectomy. Median estimated blood loss was 400cc, with 3 patients requiring postoperative transfusion. No intraoperative complications occurred, and intraoperative adhesions were noted in 16% of cases.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 (NCT03693300) on the PACIFIC-6 trial, involving 117 patients with unresectable stage III non-small-cell lung cancer treated with durvalumab after sCRT, reported that 27.4% of patients experienced grade 3/4 adverse events (AEs) and 6.0% experienced grade 3/4 possibly related AEs. Median overall survival (OS) was 39.0 months, with a 3-year OS rate of 56.5%. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was 13.1 months, with a 2-year PFS rate of 35.3%. The study concluded that durvalumab after sCRT was well tolerated and could be an alternative treatment strategy when cCRT is not feasible.
A study published in British journal of cancer in 2025 (NCT03751761) on a multicenter phase Ib/II trial evaluating durvalumab, tremelimumab, and paclitaxel as second-line treatment for biomarker-selected metastatic gastric cancer found the combination to be tolerable and efficacious. The objective response rate (ORR) was 52.1%, meeting the primary endpoint. The median progression-free survival (PFS) was 5.3 months and overall survival (OS) was 13.1 months. The most common any-grade adverse event was anemia (41.7%), and the most common grade 3-4 adverse event was neutropenia (10.4%).
A study published in Journal of hepatology in 2025 (NCT03875235) on the long-term follow-up of durvalumab plus gemcitabine and cisplatin (GemCis) in advanced biliary tract cancer (aBTC) found that survival benefit and manageable safety continued approximately 3 years after the last participant was randomized. Median OS for durvalumab+GemCis was 12.9 months vs. 11.3 months for placebo+GemCis (HR, 0.74). The 36-month OS rate was 14.6% for durvalumab+GemCis vs. 6.9% for placebo+GemCis. Overall, 12.8% of participants were extended long-term survivors (eLTS), with more in the durvalumab+GemCis arm (17.0%).
A study published in Hepatology (Baltimore, Md.) in 2026 found that participants who experienced immune-mediated adverse events (imAEs) with STRIDE (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) had a numerical improvement in OS versus those who did not, and results continue to support the benefits of STRIDE in unresectable HCC. The OS Hazard Ratio (95% CI) for STRIDE in participants with imAEs versus those without was 0.73 (0.56-0.95). The 36-month OS rate for STRIDE was 36.2% (with imAEs) and 27.7% (without imAEs). Most imAEs were low grade, manageable, and occurred within the first 3 months after treatment initiation.
A study published in Future oncology (London, England) in 2025 (NCT03043872) reported an exploratory analysis of patient-reported adverse events (AEs) using PRO-CTCAE data from a subset of 164 patients in the CASPIAN study, which evaluated durvalumab plus platinum-etoposide (EP) in extensive-stage small-cell lung cancer (ES-SCLC). The study found that AE rates and patterns reported by patients were generally comparable between the durvalumab + EP and EP arms over the first 24 weeks, with most reported AEs occurring rarely/occasionally and being mild/moderate in severity. This suggests that durvalumab improved survival without adversely affecting patients’ perspectives on side effects and quality of life.
A study published in Lung cancer (Amsterdam, Netherlands) in 2025 on the CHESS phase II trial, which enrolled 49 patients with synchronous oligometastatic non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) treated with durvalumab, carboplatin, paclitaxel, and tremelimumab, did not meet its primary endpoint of one-year progression-free survival (33% achieved vs. 50% target). However, the one-year overall survival rate was 74.9%, and treatment-related grade ≥3 adverse events occurred in 34% of patients with no grade 5 events. The favorable safety profile and promising overall survival provided the basis for further intensification of induction systemic therapy.
A study published in ESMO open in 2025 (EORTC-HNCG-1559) on the I2 substudy of the UPSTREAM trial evaluated monalizumab + durvalumab (D+M) versus physician's choice in heavily pretreated R/M SCCHN patients previously exposed to anti-PD(L)1. The study failed to demonstrate activity of D+M, with only one partial response in the D+M arm and no benefit in PFS (2.0 months vs 3.1 months in control) and OS (4.3 months vs 8.0 months in control).
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 found that the combination of neoadjuvant durvalumab and olaparib shows therapeutic activity in bladder cancer. In a phase II clinical trial involving 29 patients with resectable urothelial bladder cancer, a pathologic complete response was observed in 13 cases (44.8%), with no major safety concerns. Resistance mechanisms appeared to be driven by transcriptional adaptations rather than new mutations.
A study published in European urology oncology in 2025 found that ICI-chemotherapy combination therapy, including durvalumab, was associated with a significantly higher pathological complete response (pCR) rate (40.6%) compared to chemotherapy alone (17.9%) in neoadjuvant muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC). However, two phase 3 RCTs showed no significant difference in overall survival (OS) between durvalumab + gemcitabine + cisplatin (GC) and dose-dense methotrexate + vinblastine + Adriamycin + cisplatin (ddMVAC). ddMVAC significantly increased the risk of grade ≥3 anemia and asthenia compared to durvalumab + GC.
A study published in Gynecologic oncology in 2025 (NCT03277482) on a phase I clinical trial investigating radiation therapy (RT), durvalumab, and tremelimumab for recurrent gynecologic cancer found limited dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs), indicating acceptable safety. Zero DLTs were observed in cohort A and one DLT in cohort B. However, the overall response rate (ORR) of non-irradiated lesions was 0%, though one patient with oligometastatic platinum-resistant ovarian cancer experienced a prolonged durable benefit (>3 years).
A study published in Journal of hepatology in 2025 (NCT03298451) on the 5-year overall survival (OS) data for STRIDE (tremelimumab plus durvalumab) versus sorafenib in unresectable HCC found that STRIDE sustained an OS benefit vs. sorafenib. At 5 years, OS rates for STRIDE vs. sorafenib were 19.6% vs. 9.4% overall. STRIDE maintained a manageable safety profile with no late-onset treatment-related serious adverse events reported, and 1 in 5 patients were alive at 5 years.
A study published in Journal of thoracic oncology : official publication of the International Association for the Study of Lung Cancer in 2025 (WJOG 12119L) on durvalumab plus carboplatin/paclitaxel in resectable Stage III N2 NSCLC found that the primary endpoint, major pathologic response (MPR), was 63%, which met the target. The pathologic complete response rate was 23%. At a median follow-up of 28 months, the two-year progression-free survival (PFS) rate was 43% and the two-year overall survival (OS) rate was 76%. Grade 3 or 4 adverse events occurred in 48% of cases, including one treatment-related death. The benefit in MPR did not necessarily translate into improved PFS or OS.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 (NRG-GY023) on a randomized four-arm superiority phase II trial for platinum-resistant ovarian cancer with prior bevacizumab exposure found that all experimental arms (durvalumab + olaparib + cediranib, durvalumab + cediranib, or olaparib + cediranib) failed to reach the primary objective of improving progression-free survival (PFS) compared with standard-of-care chemotherapy. Median PFS was 3.4 months for SOC, 2.9 months for DOC, 2.5 months for DC, and 2.8 months for OC. No new safety signals were observed.
A study published in British journal of cancer in 2025 (NCT04294498) on a single-arm phase 2 clinical trial for advanced HCC patients with untreated chronic active hepatitis B, who received durvalumab alone or in combination with tremelimumab (STRIDE regimen) with simultaneous anti-HBV treatment, found no patients experienced HBV reactivation or HBV-associated hepatitis. Hepatitis flare was noted in 26.7% of patients but was not associated with HBV reactivation. Objective tumor response rate was 10% for durvalumab alone and 25% for the STRIDE regimen.
A study published in Radiotherapy and oncology : journal of the European Society for Therapeutic Radiology and Oncology in 2025 on a dosimetric analysis of 151 patients treated in the Neo-CheckRay trial (preoperative immune-modulating stereotactic body radiation therapy in combination with chemotherapy with or without durvalumab and oleclumab in early-stage, high-risk, luminal B breast cancer) found that predefined organs at risk dose constraints were feasible and the tumor draining lymph nodes (TDLN) were adequately spared. The median V95% of the gross target volume and planning target volume were 97.4% and 95.5%, respectively, and the mean dose to all TDLN volumes was < 1 Gy.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 found that neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) combined with dual immune checkpoint inhibitors (durvalumab + tremelimumab) is feasible for advanced-stage epithelial ovarian cancer (EOC) and shows promising activity with a durable clinical response. The 12-month PFS rate was 65.9%, and the 30-month overall survival rate was 87.7%. After NAC, the objective response rate was 86.7%, with 11.1% achieving pathologic complete response. The most common grade ≥3 adverse event was neutropenia (26.7%).
A study published in Gut in 2025 (NCT02821754, 2019-002767-98) on a Phase II clinical trial of tremelimumab plus durvalumab in 28 HCC patients found a median overall survival of 19.2 months. Tumour tissue analysis revealed enhanced interferon responses and antigen presentation in responders. Non-responder tumours had higher numbers of Tregs located in immune cell-enriched neighborhoods and expressed higher levels of ICOS and PD-1. The study provides a blueprint for in-depth analysis of immune correlates in immunotherapy and demonstrates the importance of Treg distribution in HCC.
A study published in Blood advances in 2025 (NCT03011814) on a Phase 1/2 clinical trial of durvalumab and lenalidomide in relapsed/refractory cutaneous T-cell lymphoma found the combination showed clinical activity with 7 partial responses and 4 stable disease, achieving best overall and skin response rates of 58.3% and 75% respectively, with a median duration of response of 25.5 months. No serious adverse events or dose-limiting toxicities were observed during early cycles, and the most frequent AEs were tumor flare, fatigue, neutropenia, and leukopenia.
A study published in Journal of clinical oncology : official journal of the American Society of Clinical Oncology in 2025 (NCT04617756) on the iNDUCT-GETUG V08 phase II trial evaluating neoadjuvant durvalumab plus gemcitabine/cisplatin (cohort 1) or carboplatin (cohort 2) in high-risk operable Upper Tract Urothelial Carcinoma (UTUC) found that the study did not meet its primary endpoint of pathologic complete response (pT0) rate (13% in cohort 1, 5% in cohort 2). However, it showed promising results in terms of downstaging (50% in cohort 1, 42% in cohort 2) and had a good safety profile with no severe immunotherapy-mediated toxicity.
A study published in Clinical cancer research : an official journal of the American Association for Cancer Research in 2025 on a Phase I study evaluating AZD8701 alone or combined with durvalumab in patients with advanced solid tumors found the combination to be clinically feasible with generally manageable adverse events. One patient treated with AZD8701 720 mg and durvalumab achieved a partial response. FOXP3 mRNA reduction was observed in 8/13 patients, and ASO accumulated in tumor epithelium and stroma.
A study published in Chinese medical journal in 2025 on a meta-analysis of 16 studies (1156 patients) comparing 6 treatment measures for unresectable stage III EGFR mutant NSCLC found that TKI-containing regimens significantly prolonged PFS compared to TKI-free regimens (HR = 0.37). RT-TKI ranked first in Bayesian analysis, showing the longest OS (60.8 months) and PFS (21.5 months). Durvalumab after chemoradiotherapy (CRT) did not show survival benefits for EGFR mutant patients in the PACIFIC study.
A meta-analysis published in The Cochrane database of systematic reviews in 2026 (raw/pubmed/atezolizumab/2026-05-25/abstract-42165290.json) involving seven RCTs with 4341 participants, including trials with durvalumab, found that combining PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy before breast cancer surgery significantly improves pathological complete response rate (RR 1.47), event-free survival (HR 0.64), and overall survival (HR 0.56) in early triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) compared to chemotherapy alone. However, this combination also probably increases immune-related serious adverse events (OR 1.75). After breast cancer surgery, there may be little or no difference in EFS and OS, but immune-related SAEs are still increased.
An observational study involving 88 patients evaluated the safety and recommended phase 2 dose of tarlatamab in combination with a PD-L1 inhibitor. The primary result was: The most common grade 3-4 adverse events were hyponatraemia (nine [10%] of 88 patients), anaemia (seven [8%] of 88 patients), and neutropenia (six [7%] of 88 patients). Serious adverse events occurred in 50 (57%) of 88 patients. The most common serious adverse events were cytokine release syndrome (21 [24%] of 88 patients), pyrexia (six [7%] of 88 patients), immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (four [5%] of 88 patients), and pneumonia (four [5%] of 88 patients). There were no deaths due to treatment-related adverse events. Median overall survival was 25.3 months (95% CI 20.3-not estimable). The study concluded: Tarlatamab plus a PD-L1 inhibitor as maintenance after first-line chemo-immunotherapy showed a manageable safety profile with promising anticancer activity, supporting the ongoing phase 3 trial (NCT06211036). A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis published in Frontiers in immunology in 2025, including 25 randomized controlled trials, compared various immune checkpoint inhibitors against chemotherapy alone for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). Cemiplimab [HR = 0.48, 95% CI: (0.34-0.67)] showed the best OS benefit, while Sugemalimab+chemo provided the best PFS benefit [HR = 0.34, 95% CI: (0.24-0.48)]. For PD-L1≥50% tumors, penpulimab showed excellent OS and PFS. For PD-L1 1-49% tumors, [[pembrolizumab]]+chemo and camrelizumab+chemo achieved the best OS and PFS, respectively. For PD-L1≥1% tumors, tislelizumab+chemo and camrelizumab+chemo showed the best OS and PFS results. For tumors with PD-L1 <1%, [[nivolumab]] and serplulimab+chemo provided significant survival benefit. In Asian patients, [[pembrolizumab]] or [[pembrolizumab]] + chemotherapy had favorable OS and PFS benefits. In non-Asian patients, cemiplimab showed favorable OS and PFS benefit. For former/current smokers, [[pembrolizumab]]+chemotherapy and camrelizumab+chemotherapy had significant OS and PFS benefit. Camrelizumab+chemo [OR = 3.5, 95% CI: (2.3-5.3)] had the best overall response rate (ORR) benefit. Ipilimumab+chemo had the highest incidence of adverse events (AEs) [OR = 2.0, 95% CI:(1.5-2.7)]. A systematic review and meta-analysis involving 3302 patients compared [[pembrolizumab]] to standard therapy, placebo, or other immune checkpoint inhibitors for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). In locally advanced disease, pembrolizumab added to CCRT showed better OS and PFS results compared to CCRT alone, and SUCRA rankings placed it above the regimen containing durvalumab. In the advanced disease setting, pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy improved OS and PFS overall compared to other ICIs, especially in patients receiving [[bevacizumab]]. Subgroup analyses suggested cadonilimab may offer superior outcomes in non-metastatic patients. Pembrolizumab ranked highest in OS and PFS among patients with locally advanced or advanced cervical cancer. These findings support its preferential use over other ICIs. However, direct head-to-head trials are needed to validate these results and inform optimal ICI selection. A meta-analysis involving 4341 patients compared PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy to chemotherapy alone for pathological complete response, event-free survival (EFS), and overall survival (OS). The primary result was: PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably increase pathological complete response rate (RR 1.47, 95% CI 1.15 to 1.86; 6 studies, 1564 participants); improve EFS (HR 0.64, 95% CI 0.52 to 0.79; 4 studies, 1789 participants); and probably improve OS (HR 0.56, 95% CI 0.34 to 0.93; 3 studies, 1681 participants) compared with chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery. Secondary results included: PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably increase immune-related SAEs (OR 1.75, 95% CI 1.15 to 2.67; 5 studies, 2016 participants) compared with chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery. There may be little or no difference between PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy and chemotherapy alone in HRQoL (MD -1.49, 95% CI -3.88 to 0.91; 2 studies, 1395 participants) before breast cancer surgery. PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy probably have little or no effect on any adverse events (OR 0.26, 95% CI 0.05 to 1.24; 3 studies, 1781 participants) and treatment-related deaths (RD 0.2%, 95% CI -0.4% to 0.8%; 4 studies, 1761 participants) before breast cancer surgery. The addition of PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors to chemotherapy probably increases immune-related serious adverse events, both before and after breast cancer surgery. The study concluded: Combining PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy compared to chemotherapy alone before breast cancer surgery improves pathological response, EFS, and OS in early TNBC. In contrast, the combination of PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors with chemotherapy after breast cancer surgery may have little to no effect on EFS and OS in early-stage TNBC when compared with chemotherapy alone. The addition of PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors probably increases immune-related SAEs. (The Cochrane database of systematic reviews, 2026) A meta-analysis published in Frontiers in immunology in 2026, involving 13572 patients, compared various ICI-based combinations against sorafenib for overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). In the overall population, regimens with significant OS advantage over sorafenib included sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar (HR = 0.57, 95% CrI 0.43-0.75), camrelizumab plus rivoceranib (HR = 0.62, 0.48-0.79), and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab (HR = 0.66, 0.51-0.84). For PFS, top-ranked combinations were camrelizumab plus rivoceranib (HR = 0.52, 0.41-0.66), anlotinib plus penpulimab (HR = 0.53, 0.41-0.68), lenvatinib plus [[pembrolizumab]] (HR = 0.55, 0.44-0.68), and sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar (HR = 0.56, 0.45-0.69). ORR was highest with lenvatinib plus [[pembrolizumab]] (RR = 8.00, 4.98-12.86). In HBV-related HCC, sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab led OS rankings, with PFS favoring cabozantinib plus [[atezolizumab]] and [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab. In HCV-related HCC, only [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab conferred a significant OS benefit (HR = 0.43, 0.25-0.73), while PFS superiority was observed only for cabozantinib plus [[atezolizumab]] (HR = 0.73, 0.54-0.99). In non-viral HCC, the STRIDE regimen (single priming dose tremelimumab plus [[durvalumab]]) was the only regimen to significantly improve OS (HR = 0.75, 0.59-0.96). Regarding safety, tislelizumab (RR = 0.42, 0.33-0.52) and [[nivolumab]] (RR = 0.45, 0.36-0.56) were associated with the lowest incidence of AEs≥3. For first-line therapy in advanced HCC, ICI-based combinations with anti-VEGF/anti-angiogenic agents generally outperform sorafenib, with discernible etiology-specific optima: HBV-related HCC favors sintilimab plus bevacizumab biosimilar or [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab; HCV-related HCC favors [[atezolizumab]] plus bevacizumab; and in non-viral disease, STRIDE demonstrates a unique OS advantage. This etiology-stratified evidence framework may guide individualized first-line decision-making, pending confirmation in head-to-head trials.
