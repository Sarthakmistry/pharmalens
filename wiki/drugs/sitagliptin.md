### Management sentiment
In Q3 2025, Januvia sales declined to $382 million from $405 million in Q3 2024. This trend continued into Q1 2026, where sales were $367 million, a decrease of 33%, further impacted by the IRA's 'Drug Price Negotiation Program' with a set price effective January 1, 2026. Management's sentiment remains bearish given these pricing pressures and ongoing sales decline.

### Clinical evidence
A Randomized Controlled Trial (UMIN000028014) involving 340 patients with T2DM, published in Diabetes, obesity & metabolism in 2025, compared [[sitagliptin]] with [[dapagliflozin]]. The primary outcome measured HbA1c and time in range. The primary result showed that Baseline ALT levels were significantly associated with changes in HbA1c and time in range at 24 weeks. Patients with lower ALT showed better response to sitagliptin, while those with higher ALT exhibited enhanced efficacy with dapagliflozin across all models (p < 0.05). Regression analysis revealed significant interactions between ALT, BMI and HbA1c (p = 0.02, 0.03). A significant correlation between baseline ALT and HbA1c change was observed in the total cohort (p = 0.03) and sitagliptin group (p < 0.01). The study concluded: Baseline ALT is a potential predictor of differential efficacy between dapagliflozin and sitagliptin in T2DM, independent of BMI. Lower ALT correlated with enhanced sitagliptin efficacy, whereas higher ALT favoured dapagliflozin for glycaemic control. A recent Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) published in Advances in therapy in 2025 investigated the efficacy and safety of a triple fixed-dose combination (FDC) of [[dapagliflozin]], [[sitagliptin]], and metformin IR compared to a dual FDC of [[sitagliptin]] and metformin IR for patients with [[type2-diabetes]]. The primary outcome was the mean change in glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c) from baseline to Week 16. The primary result showed that Adjusted mean reduction in HbA1c from baseline to Week 16 was significantly greater in triple FDC of DAPA + SITA + MET IR (- 2.08%) versus dual FDC of SITA + MET IR (- 1.38%) P < 0.0001. A significantly greater reduction in HbA1c was observed with triple FDC compared to the dual FDC at Week 12 (P < 0.0001). At Week 16, a significantly higher proportion of patients achieved HbA1c < 7% with triple FDC than with dual FDC (48.9% versus 31.1%; P = 0.0029). Both treatments were well tolerated. There were no major safety concerns. The study concluded: Triple FDC of DAPA + SITA + MET IR demonstrated superior efficacy in achieving glycaemic control in patients with T2DM compared to dual FDC. There were no major safety concerns. A Randomized Controlled Trial (KCT0009925) published in the Journal of Korean medical science in 2026, involving 34 participants, compared [[dapagliflozin]] to [[sitagliptin]] with body weight changes from baseline as the primary outcome. Weight reduction in the dapagliflozin group was lower than expected, with a 1.99 kg difference at 24 weeks (P < 0.001). Leptin levels significantly decreased in the dapagliflozin group. Food intake and appetite scores showed no differences between the groups. Food intake at 12 weeks of [[empagliflozin]] treatment in OLETF rats showed no difference from the controls, while increased intake during the first 3 weeks in the empagliflozin group. SGLT2 inhibitors support metabolic safety with respect to energy intake by not stimulating appetite or affecting appetite-regulating molecules. Sustained SGLT2 inhibitor treatment does not cause compensatory appetite increase after weight stabilization. Furthermore, it does not stimulate appetite or affect appetite-regulating molecules, thereby supporting its metabolic safety with respect to energy intake. This finding suggests that less-than-expected weight loss with SGLT2 inhibitors may result from mechanisms other than sustained changes in appetite or food intake.
A Randomized Controlled Trial published in Molecular metabolism in 2026 investigated ARD-101 (denatonium acetate), a gut-restricted TAS2R agonist, for obesity. In DIO mice, the combination of ARD-101 and [[sitagliptin]] reduced body weight by -18.8% and limited weight regain comparable to continued [[tirzepatide]] after its discontinuation. The study concluded that gut-restricted TAS2R agonism warrants further study, particularly in combination with DPP-4 inhibition for obesity.
A meta-analysis published in BMC medicine in 2026, including 55 RCTs and 16,610 participants, compared various anti-prediabetic drugs. While most drugs significantly reduced HbA1c, FPG, weight loss, and BMI, 100 mg of [[sitagliptin]] demonstrated a higher incidence of serious adverse events (SAEs) compared to other interventions. The study highlighted GLP-1RAs and GIP/GLP-1RAs as having favorable efficacy and acceptable safety.
A Randomized Controlled Trial published in the International journal of molecular sciences in 2025, involving 34 patients with T2DM, compared [[semaglutide]] with [[sitagliptin]]. [[Semaglutide]] significantly reduced BMI, waist circumference, and HbA1c, and improved lipid parameters, redistributing LDL and HDL subfractions toward a less atherogenic profile. In contrast, [[sitagliptin]] showed modest glycemic improvements but no substantial lipid alterations. The study concluded that these results support the pleiotropic metabolic benefits of semaglutide and its potential role in managing cardiometabolic risk in T2DM.
A Randomized Controlled Trial (NCT03257232, NCT03257258) published in Diabetes, obesity & metabolism in 2025, involving 1474 Chinese participants with type 2 diabetes, compared oral [[semaglutide]] to placebo or [[sitagliptin]] 100mg. Oral semaglutide significantly reduced HbA1c and body weight across various baseline subgroups, with greater reductions in HbA1c observed in subgroups with higher baseline HbA1c. Most adverse events were gastrointestinal-related and mild-to-moderate. The study concluded that oral semaglutide reduces HbA1c and body weight without unexpected safety concerns, supporting its use in a broad population.
