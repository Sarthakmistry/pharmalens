## Alectinib Trial NCT02075840 Completed; Meta-analysis Highlights Significant Under-reported Weight Gain

**Date:** 2017-02-09 | **Type:** trial_completion | **Signal:** bearish

### Summary
Trial NCT02075840, investigating alectinib for oncology-nsclc, reached primary completion on February 9, 2017. A subsequent meta-analysis of alectinib studies revealed that weight gain is significantly under-reported as an adverse event, with 18% of patients experiencing ≥10% weight gain, suggesting a need for increased clinical awareness and monitoring.

### Market implication
The findings on under-reported weight gain for alectinib could lead to increased scrutiny of its safety profile and potentially impact prescribing patterns if not adequately addressed.

### Clinical evidence
A meta-analysis of alectinib studies (J Clin Oncol, 2025) involving 302 patients found that time on alectinib was positively associated with weight change (β = .37; 95% CI, 0.24 to 0.51; P < .001), corresponding to an average increase of 4.4% over 1 year. 18% of patients experienced ≥10% weight gain (from median 55.6 kg to 64.1 kg). Actual weight gain of any grade was substantially higher than reported AE rates (49% v 5%). At baseline, 13.6% of the Japanese population were underweight and 5.0% in the Western population. Baseline BMI was not associated with weight change in J-ALEX (β = -.090 [95% CI, -0.19 to 0.012]; P = .092) and ALUR/ML29453 (β = -.016 [95% CI, -0.077 to 0.044]; P = .59). Baseline albumin was positively associated with weight change in ALUR/ML29453 (β = .084 [95% CI, 0.027 to 0.14]; P = .0045), although not considered a clinically meaningful predictor. Weight gain is under-reported as AE in trials. Actual weights showed ≥10% weight gain in 18% of patients. Clinicians should be aware of this AE, emphasizing the importance of timely identification and monitoring weight. Identifying predictors for weight gain remains challenging.
